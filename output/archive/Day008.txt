FILM-PHOTOGRAPHY
----------------

Topic

Composition Essentials for Film Shots

Lesson

Why Composition Matters on Film
Film rewards intention. You cannot preview the exact result on the back of the camera, and you have a finite number of frames. Composition is your way to decide what earns a place on that small rectangle. Good framing guides the eye, clarifies the subject, and makes the negative worth developing.

First Principles: Figure–Ground and Signal–Noise
Composition is managing attention. Figure–ground means the subject (figure) stands out against everything else (ground). If figure and ground blend, viewers hunt for meaning and tire quickly. Signal-to-noise is the ratio of meaningful information (signal) to distractions (noise). You raise signal by choosing a clear subject and lighting or positioning it to stand out. You reduce noise by removing clutter, simplifying the background, and excluding anything that does not support the subject.

Core Tools
1) Rule of thirds: Imagine a 3x3 grid in the frame. Place important elements near the intersections or along grid lines. This creates balance and tension. It is a guide, not a law. Centering can be powerful for symmetry or portraits with direct gaze.
2) Leading lines: Roads, rails, fences, shorelines, and shadows act like arrows. Align them to draw the eye to your subject. Converging lines can add depth, especially in 35mm where the frame is wide and invites perspective.
3) Framing: Use doorways, windows, foliage, or arches to create a frame within the frame. This both isolates the subject and adds layers.
4) Negative space and balance: Leave deliberate empty space to emphasize small subjects or to suggest mood. Balance visual weight. A bright object near the edge can counterweight a darker subject near a third.
5) Foreground, middle, background: Give the viewer a path in. A near object adds depth, a middle subject anchors attention, and a background provides context. On film, this helps B&W especially, where tonal layers matter.
6) Edge patrol: Before you click, sweep your eye around the borders of the frame. Cut off distractions, avoid half objects, and keep strong lines from exiting awkwardly.

Simplify Before You Click
Move your feet. Most composition problems vanish when you change height, angle, or distance. Step left to clear a lamppost from a head. Kneel to hide clutter behind a subject with a clean sky. Turn your subject slightly toward the light to separate them from the background. If you shoot color negative film, use color contrast to create figure–ground (red clothing against green foliage). In B&W, look for tonal contrast and shape. Prefer fewer, stronger elements over many weak ones. Ask: What can I remove and still say the same thing?

Work with Fixed Frame Counts
A roll of 36 frames (or 12 on medium format) forces choices. Previsualize: imagine the final print before raising the camera. Anticipate timing and commit to the best moment, not a spray of near-duplicates. If you want variants, bracket composition, not just exposure: one with more negative space, one tighter, one from a lower angle. But limit yourself: three tries per idea keeps you intentional. Think in sequences: opening wide shot for context, medium for subject, close for detail. This helps you tell a story across frames and pick a keeper later.

A Fast Composition Loop
- Choose a single, clear subject: person, shape, gesture, texture, or light.
- Establish figure–ground: separate by light, color, focus, or size.
- Simplify: remove one distracting element (move, reframe, wait for a gap in traffic).
- Arrange: use thirds, lines, and framing to guide the eye.
- Edge patrol: check corners and borders for intrusions.
- Balance and negative space: is the frame weighted as you intend?
- Time the moment: gesture, expression, or alignment.
- Commit: press the shutter with confidence.
This loop takes seconds with practice and respects the cost of each frame.

Breaking the Rules On Purpose
- Centering: Use it for symmetry, strength, or direct confrontation.
- Horizon placement: Place horizon low to emphasize sky and mood, high to emphasize texture of land or water.
- Silhouettes: Expose for the background to reduce the subject to shape. Figure–ground by outline.
- Fill the frame: Eliminate context for graphic power. Works well for textures and faces.
Break rules when the break strengthens the idea. The composition should serve a feeling or message, not a checklist.

Light and Lenses
Composition rides on light. Side light reveals shape; backlight separates; flat light reduces contrast and may ask for stronger lines or patterns. Wide lenses exaggerate perspective and invite leading lines; telephoto compresses space and simplifies backgrounds. Use these tendencies to control signal and noise.

Decide What the Photo Is About
Before you shoot, finish this sentence: This photo is about _____. If you cannot answer, wait. Film teaches patience. When you do answer, everything else in the frame should support that answer.

Example

You arrive at a seaside pier. The subject is a lone fisherman in a yellow jacket. You think: This photo is about quiet patience. You move left so the railing forms a leading line from the bottom-right corner toward him. You crouch to place the horizon on the upper third, keeping sky minimal to reduce empty brightness. The yellow jacket contrasts with the cool blue water, giving figure–ground. You use the doorway of a bait shop as a foreground frame, darkening the edges. Edge patrol: you shift a step so a trash can leaves the frame. You wait for the fisherman to cast, so the rod points along the railing’s line. Click. One frame. Then you make a tighter variant with more negative space to the right, inviting the eye to rest on the water.

Scenario

You are traveling with one 36-exposure roll on a foggy morning in a hill town. You set a simple plan: 6 scenes, up to 6 frames each. Scene 1 is the market square. You scout first: bright fruit stalls, messy background wires, people milling. You choose a stallholder as your subject and step to align a string of hanging lights as a leading line into her workspace. You lower your angle so the far wall becomes a clean background. You wait for a customer to lean in, creating a triangle of faces. You take two frames at that peak moment, then move on. Later, reviewing contact sheets, you see that the patient, composed frames beat the earlier, busier attempts. The constraint forced you to simplify and to time your moments.

Reflection Question

When you look at your recent photos, what recurring distractions (clutter, mergers, weak figure–ground) keep diluting your subject, and what one habit change would most reduce that noise?

MICROECONOMICS
--------------

Topic

Markets and Prices—What Do They Do?

Lesson

First Principles: Scarcity and Coordination
Resources are limited, wants are many, and knowledge is scattered across millions of people. No single person knows the exact costs, preferences, or constraints of everyone else. Markets solve this coordination problem with a simple device: prices. A price condenses information about scarcity and desire into one number that anyone can read and act on.

Price as Signal
A price tells you something about relative scarcity. When the price of avocados rises, it signals that demand is high, supply is tight, or both. This signal ripples out: retailers adjust shelf space, restaurants tweak menus, farmers consider planting more next season, and shoppers decide whether to buy less or substitute. The beauty is that no central planner needs to send detailed instructions; the price carries the message.

Price as Incentive
Signals matter only if people have reason to respond. Prices pay people to create more of what is scarce and to use less of what is costly. Higher prices reward sellers to produce and bring goods to market; they also encourage innovation and entry. Lower prices tell buyers to consume more and sellers to find cheaper ways to produce or to exit. Profit and loss are feedback: they encourage effective plans and discourage ineffective ones.

Relative Prices and Trade-offs
What matters is not a price in isolation, but prices relative to alternatives. If chicken prices rise while beans stay flat, some households shift protein sources. Relative prices steer resources toward their highest valued use. This is marginal thinking in action: decisions are made at the edge, comparing the next unit’s benefit to its cost.

Coordination Without Central Control
Markets coordinate through adjustment. If there is excess demand at a current price, buyers bid up the price, sellers increase quantity supplied, and the gap closes. If there is excess supply, prices fall and sellers cut output. Over time, expectations adjust, inventories buffer shocks, and capacity expands or contracts. Futures markets extend this coordination across time by letting people trade today on expectations about tomorrow.

When Prices Work Less Well
- Externalities: When my action affects others without being priced (pollution, vaccine spillovers), market prices miss part of the true cost or benefit. Quantity can be too high (negative externalities) or too low (positive externalities).
- Market power: Monopolies or tight oligopolies can set prices above competitive levels, reducing output and creating deadweight loss.
- Information problems: Buyers or sellers may know more than the other side (used cars, health insurance). Adverse selection and moral hazard can distort outcomes.
- Public goods: Non-excludable and non-rival goods (national defense, lighthouse signals) are underprovided by markets because free riders can benefit without paying.
- Transaction costs and thin markets: When it is hard to find partners, write contracts, or enforce them, exchange may not occur even when it is efficient.
- Price controls: Caps can create shortages; floors can create surpluses. They also distort quality margins and non-price rationing (lines, favoritism).
These are invitations to careful policy design, not a dismissal of prices.

Reading Prices in Practice
Ask three questions: What shock could have moved this price (demand, supply, or both)? How elastic are responses in the short run versus long run? What margins will adjust first (quantity, quality, timing, location)? A short-run spike often draws on inventories and delays nonessential uses; long-run changes shift investment and technology.

Fairness, Distribution, and Design
Markets are powerful at creating coordination and growth; they are silent about distribution. Two societies can have the same prices and production but allocate gains very differently. We often layer taxes, transfers, and rules on top of markets to address distribution and failures while trying to preserve the coordinating role of prices.

A Simple Heuristic
- If a price rises, ask: Who is being told to conserve? Who is being paid to supply more?
- If a price is capped, ask: What will start to be rationed by something other than money (time, connections, quality)?
- If a price seems wrong, ask: Is there missing information or a missing market? Could we create one (e.g., congestion or carbon pricing)?
Prices are a language; fluency means you can predict how people will reply.

Example

A drought hits a major coffee-growing region. Futures prices for arabica beans jump. What happens? Farmers with irrigation keep trees alive and consider planting more next season. Some marginal farms exit if costs soar. Exporters reroute limited supply to buyers who pay more reliably. Roasters hedge, reformulate blends with more robusta, or offer smaller bag sizes. Cafes raise prices a little, push drip over expensive single-origin pour-overs, and promote food combos to maintain average ticket size. Consumers switch to home brewing or drink coffee fewer days per week. Six months later, if rains return and new plantings mature, prices ease and blends shift back. The price rise coordinated conservation, substitution, and new supply across thousands of uncoordinated decisions.

Scenario

A city considers congestion pricing: charge cars more to enter downtown during peak hours. The price is a signal that road space is scarce at 8–10am. Incentives shift: some commuters carpool or take transit; companies stagger work hours; delivery firms reschedule routes; ride-hailing pools trips. Traffic speed increases for those who pay, buses become faster, and emissions fall. But equity concerns surface: lower-income drivers with inflexible hours bear more burden. The city uses the revenue to improve transit in affected neighborhoods and discounts the fee for residents with verified need. The policy uses price to coordinate behavior while adding design features to address fairness.

Reflection Question

Think of a price you see each week that seems too high or too low. If you could change that price today, what adjustments across buyers and sellers would you expect tomorrow, next month, and next year—and what unintended consequences might follow?

MICROSOFT 365 ADMINISTRATOR (MS-102)
------------------------------------

Topic

Core security and compliance concepts in Microsoft 365

Lesson

First Principles: Zero Trust in Microsoft 365
Zero Trust starts from three ideas: verify explicitly, use least privilege, and assume breach. Trust is not a location (like an office network); it is a calculation using signals: user identity, device state, location, app sensitivity, data classification, and real-time risk. In Microsoft 365, Entra ID (formerly Azure AD) is the control plane for identity and access. Conditional Access evaluates signals at sign-in and during sessions to decide whether to allow, block, or require stronger controls (like MFA or device compliance). Least privilege means giving users the minimum access needed and elevating only when required, ideally with time-bound approval using Privileged Identity Management (PIM). Assuming breach means segmenting administration, monitoring continuously, and planning for containment and recovery.

Shared Responsibility in the Cloud
With Microsoft 365 (a SaaS platform), Microsoft secures the underlying infrastructure, data centers, service code, and availability. You control identities, access policies, configuration, data, endpoints, and compliance obligations. In short: Microsoft runs the service; you run your tenant. That includes enabling MFA, setting Conditional Access rules, assigning roles, labeling data, configuring Data Loss Prevention, and meeting legal holds and retention requirements. Microsoft provides resilience and recycle bins, but you must set retention and backup strategies that meet your business and regulatory needs. You are also responsible for third-party app permissions and device management (via Intune) if you want device-based controls.

Core Security Controls to Anchor On
- Strong identity: Enable MFA for all users, and require phishing-resistant methods for admins where possible. Block legacy authentication. Use Conditional Access baselines: require MFA outside trusted conditions, restrict access to compliant devices for sensitive apps, and apply sign-in risk policies.
- Least privilege and admin hygiene: Assign built-in roles narrowly; avoid permanent Global Admin. Use PIM for just-in-time elevation. Separate admin accounts from user accounts and segment duties (identity, Exchange, SharePoint, Security). Monitor role assignments.
- Device and app controls: Enforce device compliance via Intune (encryption, password, OS health). Use app protection policies for BYOD, restricting copy/paste and requiring app PINs. Combine with Conditional Access so sensitive data only lands on healthy devices or protected apps.
- Threat protection: Defender for Office 365 adds Safe Links and Safe Attachments to filter phishing and malware. Defender for Endpoint brings endpoint detection and response and integrates with Conditional Access via device risk. Use Attack Simulation to train users. Enable audit and alerting.
- Visibility and posture: Review Microsoft Secure Score to prioritize hardening steps with impact estimates. Use Identity Secure Score for identity-specific improvements.

Core Compliance and Data Governance
- Classify and protect data with Microsoft Purview Information Protection. Create sensitivity labels (e.g., Public, Internal, Confidential) that apply encryption, access restrictions, and visual markings. Labels travel with the data.
- Prevent leakage with Data Loss Prevention policies in Exchange, SharePoint, OneDrive, and Teams. Define rules for sensitive info types (like credit card numbers) and locations where sharing is restricted or warned.
- Manage lifecycle with retention labels and policies. Set default retention on key locations and apply event-based retention for records. Use Litigation Hold or Preservation policies when required for eDiscovery.
- Investigate with Purview eDiscovery (Standard/Premium). Search, hold, review, and export content with audit trails. Insider Risk Management and Communication Compliance detect risky behavior and policy violations with privacy controls.
- Compliance Manager: Map controls to standards (e.g., ISO 27001, GDPR), track implementation, and assign improvement actions with evidence collection.

A Practical Order of Operations
1) Identity first: Turn on MFA, block legacy auth, and deploy baseline Conditional Access. Protect admin accounts and enable PIM.
2) Device and session: Require compliant devices for sensitive resources. Set app protection for mobile. Use session controls for web access (download restrictions) when needed.
3) Data: Roll out simple sensitivity labels and auto-labeling for obvious patterns. Pilot DLP with audit-only mode, then enforce.
4) Lifecycle: Establish retention defaults for email and SharePoint. Implement Litigation Hold processes.
5) Threat protection: Enable Safe Links/Attachments, tune phishing thresholds, and integrate Defender for Endpoint.
6) Monitor and iterate: Use Secure Score and Compliance Manager to guide next steps and track evidence.

Mental Model to Keep
Trust is a dynamic score, not a yes/no switch. Your job is to gather reliable signals, evaluate them consistently, and fail safely when uncertain.

Example

You secure a 500-person tenant in 30 days. Week 1: Enable MFA tenant-wide using number match, block legacy protocols, create a Conditional Access policy that requires MFA for all cloud apps except break-glass accounts, and another that requires compliant devices for SharePoint and OneDrive. Week 2: Create Privileged Identity Management with approval and 1-hour elevation for Global Admin, split admin duties, and move helpdesk to Password Administrator. Week 3: Deploy Defender for Office 365 Safe Links and Safe Attachments in Monitor, review hits, then Enforce. Turn on audit and create alerts for mass download in OneDrive. Week 4: Publish sensitivity labels (Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted) with basic encryption for Restricted, start a DLP policy that warns on external sharing of Restricted docs, and set 7-year retention for Finance mailboxes. Secure Score increases, phishing success drops, and you now have consistent data controls.

Scenario

Legal asks you to preserve and collect communications for a departing VP under investigation. You place the mailbox and Teams content on Litigation Hold using Purview, ensuring nothing can be permanently deleted. You run a targeted eDiscovery search scoped to date range and keywords, preview results to remove obvious false positives, then export to Legal’s review workspace. In parallel, you verify Conditional Access prevents the ex-employee’s personal device from syncing OneDrive and disable sessions across the tenant. You document each step in Compliance Manager to build an evidence trail for potential regulators. The shared responsibility boundary is clear: Microsoft keeps the service available; you ensured identities, holds, and policies were correctly applied.

Reflection Question

Which single gap in your tenant (identity, device, data, or monitoring) would most reduce risk if fixed this week, and what minimal set of Zero Trust controls would you deploy to close it?

WISDOM
------

Topic

Principles and Values

Lesson

First Principles: What Principles Are For
Values are what you care about; principles are the rules you use to act on those values when stakes or pressure rise. Good principles compress wisdom into short, testable guidance that travels well across situations. They reduce decision fatigue, prevent drift under stress, and make trade-offs explicit. The aim is not to script life, but to set guardrails that keep you aligned when your future self is tired, rushed, or tempted.

Why Few Beats Many
Working memory is limited. Three to five principles can be remembered, rehearsed, and actually used. More than that becomes wallpaper. Each principle should be specific enough to guide a real choice, but general enough to cover repeated patterns.

From Values to Principles
1) Inventory recurring decisions: time allocation, health, money, relationships, work quality, ethics. Note where you often hesitate or regret.
2) Name the underlying values: e.g., integrity, learning, health, family, craftsmanship, service.
3) Draft principles in an if–then–because form: When X, I will Y, because Z. The because ties back to your values and reminds you why the rule exists.
4) Define scope and triggers: Where does this apply? What starts it? Include 1–2 examples to make it concrete.
5) Set priority and tie-breakers: If two principles conflict, which wins? You can add a meta-principle like Default to reversible decisions when uncertain.
6) Test in the wild: Apply for a week. After each decision, ask: Did this principle help? Too vague? Too strict? Revise.

Traits of Useful Principles
- Actionable: It tells you what to do now, not what to feel later.
- Memorable: Short, plain words you can recall under stress.
- Resilient: Holds up when it costs you something.
- Measurable: You can tell after the fact if you followed it.
- Aligned: It reflects your real values, not other people’s expectations.

Linking Principles to Recurring Choices
- Time: Block the first 90 minutes for deep work; meetings go after lunch. Because attention is scarcest in the morning.
- Health: No screens in bed; walk during 1-on-1s. Because sleep and movement multiply every other effort.
- Money: Save a fixed percentage first; avoid debt for depreciating items. Because flexibility beats fragility.
- Relationships: Praise in public, critique in private, and never let a thank-you go unsent. Because trust compounds.
- Craft: Ship a small version weekly; refactor before adding features. Because feedback beats speculation.
Map each to real triggers on your calendar, inbox, or shopping cart.

Dealing with Conflicts
Principles sometimes collide: honesty vs. kindness, speed vs. quality. You can handle this with:
- Ordering: Integrity outranks convenience.
- Timeboxing: Move fast within a safe sandbox; slow down at irreversible gates.
- Thresholds: Minimum viable quality before speed; above the threshold, optimize for time.
- Explicit exceptions: Define rare conditions that allow deviation and how you will limit the risk.

Making Them Stick
- Externalize: Write principles where you work. Turn them into checklists or calendar defaults.
- Precommit: Automate where possible (savings autopay, screen time limits, recurring workout invites).
- Socialize: Tell trusted colleagues or family; invite them to hold you to your rules.
- Review: Monthly, keep–cut–change. Prune redundant or aspirational ones you never follow.

Anti-Principles to Avoid
- Vague slogans: Be better is not a principle.
- Perfection traps: Always is brittle; prefer usually with bright-line exceptions.
- Borrowed principles: Adopted for image rather than fit; they crumble under cost.

A Simple Template
When I face [recurring situation], I will [action], because [value]. Scope: [where it applies]. Example: [one-hot scenario]. Priority: [its rank vs. others].

The Point
Principles let you decide once and act many times. Good ones lower stress, speed choices, and keep you consistent when it matters most.

Example

A product manager drafts five principles: 1) First deep work: From 9–10:30am, no meetings or Slack; plan and write specs, because clarity prevents rework. 2) Evidence over opinions: If a debate stalls, run a 48-hour experiment, because data buys alignment. 3) Protect users: No dark patterns, because trust is the moat. 4) One yes per week: Say yes to only one new commitment, because focus wins. 5) Health is fuel: Exercise before screens on weekdays, because energy compounds. Monday arrives: a senior exec invites a 9am meeting. Principle 1 triggers a polite decline with an alternative at 11am. Later, two teams argue over a feature; Principle 2 prompts a quick A/B test plan. A marketing idea proposes a misleading opt-out; Principle 3 kills it. On Thursday, three people ask for help; Principle 4 selects only the most leveraged request. The manager leaves work with a shipped spec and enough energy for an evening run, living Principle 5.

Scenario

You receive a lucrative job offer from a company with a reputation for cutting corners. Your draft principles include Protect users, Integrity over convenience, and Family time after 6pm. You map the decision: If I accept, will I likely violate Protect users? Conversations with employees suggest pressure to ship before adequate testing. Integrity over convenience would be threatened weekly. The role demands late-night availability, conflicting with Family time after 6pm. You counter with conditions: explicit authority to delay launches for safety, written support for testing gates, and a guardrail on after-hours work. The company declines. Guided by your principles, you walk away and continue the search with clearer filters.

Reflection Question

Which past decision still bothers you, and what single, well-phrased principle could have made the right choice obvious in that moment?

FICTION-WRITING
---------------

Topic

Show vs Tell and Sensory Detail

Lesson

First Principles: What Showing and Telling Do
Story is experience arranged in time. Readers want enough evidence to feel and infer, and enough summary to move forward. Showing presents concrete cues—action, dialogue, setting, sensation—so readers draw conclusions. Telling states the conclusion directly—labels, judgments, summaries. Both are tools. Show when emotion is high, stakes shift, or character changes; tell when you must compress time, deliver low-stakes facts, or guide focus without lingering.

The Show–Tell Spectrum
Think of a slider rather than a switch. On one end: pure scene—sensory detail, dialogue, gestures, subtext. In the middle: selective showing with a few telling beats for clarity. On the far end: summary—days pass, a relationship cools, the team loses five games in a row. Skilled prose moves the slider on purpose, according to what the reader must feel or know right now.

Specificity Ladder
Abstract: She was nervous. Specific: Her palms were damp. Concrete and owned by POV: The key wriggled in the lock; breath caught; the hallway’s bulb hummed too loud. Climb down the ladder when you want immersion. One vivid, accurate detail can do more than three generic ones. Prefer strong nouns and verbs to stacks of adjectives.

Sensory Detail that Works
Use a limited, precise set of details tied to your point-of-view character. Mix senses: smell anchors memory; sound carries threat; temperature and texture ground the body. Avoid catalogues. Choose the telling detail that implies the rest: the coffee stain on the CEO’s cuff suggests a bad morning; the cinnamon in the air says bakery without a sign. Keep metaphors in character: a sailor thinks in knots and tides, not circuit boards.

Showing Interior Life
You can show inner states without naming them by using:
- Action under pressure: Fumbling keys, choosing the back row, deleting an unsent text.
- Dialogue with subtext: I’m fine uttered too quickly.
- Physiological cues: Jaw muscles tic; a laugh that doesn’t reach the eyes.
- Thought snippets: Not a diary dump, just the next thought that would flash. Keep it specific and patterned to the character.

When Telling Is Better
- Transitions: Two weeks later, the trail went cold.
- Low-stakes facts: He was thirty-two and new to the city.
- Avoiding tedium: They haggled for hours.
- Orienting judgment: It was the kind of town where shutters close early.
Telling can also steer interpretation when needed, but use it sparingly at emotional peaks.

A Practical Revision Pass
1) Mark abstractions: words like sad, angry, beautiful, very, suddenly. Decide which few deserve to be shown.
2) Swap filters: Cut felt, noticed, saw when possible; describe the stimulus directly. Instead of She felt cold, try Wind licked through her coat.
3) Upgrade nouns and verbs: trade sat down for folded into the chair; walked for limped, drifted, shouldered through.
4) Choose one telling detail per beat: The fridge’s light stuttered twice.
5) Balance pace: If a paragraph bogs down, move the slider toward telling or cut.

Common Pitfalls
- Purple prose: Overwriting with lush detail that doesn’t serve the moment.
- Camera trap: Only visual detail, no body or thought.
- Stage directions: He stood, turned, walked, opened—skip the obvious.
- Over-showing trivia: Spend your ink where the stakes are.

Rule of Thumb
Show turning points and emotional hinges. Tell travel, paperwork, and calendar flips. Use sensory detail like seasoning—enough to flavor, not to bury the dish.

Example

Telling to start: She was nervous about the interview. She wanted the job. She worried she would ramble.
Revised with selective showing: The key jittered in the lock twice before it caught. In the bathroom mirror, a single hair refused to lie flat. She rehearsed the first sentence once, then again, then shut her mouth before momentum carried her into a monologue. On the train, a man’s tie was printed with tiny rockets; she decided that meant luck.
Balanced final version: She needed this job. The key jittered in the lock twice before it caught. On the train, she mouthed her opener once and let the rest stay unspoken, saving words for the room.

Scenario

You have a draft scene where two siblings argue about selling the family house. In revision, you mark telling lines: He was angry. She was stubborn. You convert only the pivotal ones to showing. He keeps wiping the same clean fork with his thumb. She folds the property survey until the crease is knife-sharp. Dialogue carries subtext: It’s just wood and nails, he says, staring at the window his father repaired three times. Fine, sell it, she says, not moving her suitcase from the hall. You keep a few tells to pace: They went in circles for another hour before the agent arrived.

Reflection Question

In your current draft, identify one high-stakes moment that deserves showing and one low-stakes span that would read better as a quick tell—how will you adjust the slider in each?
