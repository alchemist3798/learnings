FILM-PHOTOGRAPHY
----------------

Topic

Bracketing and Test Shots on Film

Lesson

First principles: uncertainty and sampling
Film records light once. You cannot chimp a histogram. So you manage uncertainty the way scientists do: by sampling. Bracketing is sampling exposure around your best estimate. Test shots are your controlled samples that reduce future uncertainty. Together they form a feedback loop: make a guess, take measured variations, read the results, refine your next guess.

Film latitude basics
Not all films forgive error equally.
- Negative film (color or B&W): generous highlight latitude. It prefers slight overexposure. Over by 1 stop is often fine; under by 1 stop can look thin.
- Slide (reversal) film: narrow latitude. It punishes both over and under. Accuracy matters; bracketing is more important.
- High-contrast scenes: latitude shrinks because the subject brightness range squeezes the film.

Designing efficient brackets
Waste comes from bracketing every frame blindly. Efficiency comes from targeting uncertainty.
- Start with a base exposure decision. Meter the key mid-tone or use incident metering. For common color negative stocks, many photographers rate the film slower than box speed (e.g., Portra 400 at EI 200) to give a safety cushion to the shadows.
- Choose a bracket step that matches risk:
  - Color negative: 1 stop steps are usually enough (0, +1). Only add a -1 frame if highlights are very important and you mistrust the meter.
  - Slide: 1/3 to 1/2 stop steps across a narrower range (e.g., -1/3, 0, +1/3).
- Bracket selectively:
  - Use single exposure for even light you trust.
  - Bracket when light is contrasty, backlit, or fast-changing; when the subject is a once-only moment; or when you are testing a new meter/film/lab.
- Keep bracket runs short. Typical run: three frames. If the scene is static and critical, five frames can be justified.

Gray cards and test frames
A gray card is a known reference. Use it to calibrate, not to fill the roll with references.
- At the start of a roll, take one controlled test:
  1) Place the gray card in the scene light, filling the frame.
  2) Meter properly (incident or reflective with care) and expose one frame at the meter reading.
  3) Add one frame at +2/3 or +1 if shooting negative film. For slide, add ±1/3.
- Purpose: When the negatives or scans return, you can judge how your meter, your chosen EI, and the lab’s processing/scanning interact.
- When the light changes drastically (e.g., golden hour to shade), add a new gray card pair.

Interpreting results
Think densities, not just prettiness.
- For negatives: Look at the density of shadows in a light table or at scan histograms. If shadows are too thin at meter reading but healthy at +1, overexpose next time by +2/3 to +1 as your baseline EI.
- For highlights: If sky or white shirts block up (no detail) at +1, consider metering highlights more carefully or adding a -1 frame when highlights matter.
- For slide: Pick the frame that preserves both highlight sparkle and shadow separation. Note the bracket step that hit the sweet spot and bias your future metering by that amount.
- Record everything. Frame numbers, bracket pattern, meter method, time of day, and lab used. Your notes turn individual rolls into a learning dataset.

Smart bracketing patterns
- Negative film, portrait in backlight: 0, +1, sometimes -1 if rim-lit hair detail is precious.
- Slide film, landscape at sunset: -1/3, 0, +1/3, maybe +2/3 if foreground is very dark.
- Interiors with window light (neg): spot meter the face, expose at reading and +1. Consider a separate shot metered for the window if you plan to blend in darkroom or scanning adjustments.

Minimizing waste in the field
- Previsualize the keeper. Ask: what detail must survive? If it’s highlights, skew bracket slightly under. If it’s skin tone, skew slightly over (for negative film).
- Use micro-brackets when time/film is tight: two frames only (0 and +2/3 for neg; 0 and -1/3 for slide).
- Don’t bracket for the sake of it. If you have solid, repeatable metering in flat light, trust it and save frames for changing light.

Building a feedback loop
- Run one calibration roll per new film/lab/meter combo. Shoot the same scene with controlled brackets and a gray card, then study the results.
- Convert your findings into a rule of thumb (e.g., “Portra 400 at EI 250; faces +2/3 in backlight”).
- Revisit your rules when seasons, labs, or cameras change.

Mindset
Bracketing is not a crutch; it’s disciplined sampling. Test frames are not waste; they are investments that reduce future waste. The goal is not perfection on each frame but a rising baseline of confidence that lets you spend film where it matters most.

Example

You’re shooting a friend’s portrait on Portra 400 in late afternoon backlight. You meter her cheek in open shade and get 1/250 at f/4 (EI 200 baseline). You take three frames: 1) 1/250 f/4 (0), 2) 1/125 f/4 (+1), 3) 1/500 f/4 (-1) because her hair rim-light is important. At the start of the roll, you shot a gray card at 1/250 f/4 and at 1/160 f/4 (+2/3). When scans return, you see the +1 frame gives rich skin and printable highlights, while the -1 keeps hair sparkle but loses cheek depth. You adopt a new rule: in backlight, shoot the face at +2/3 and add a -1 safety when highlights are critical.

Scenario

You have one roll of 36 for a travel day with mixed light: markets, interiors, rooftops at sunset. You decide: no bracketing in overcast alleys you can meter cleanly. Micro-bracket (0, +2/3) in interiors with window light. At sunset on a rooftop (high contrast), run a careful three-shot bracket (-1/3, 0, +1/3) on slide film. You take one gray card pair around midday when the sun emerges. Your notes map frame numbers to bracket steps. Later, the lab results show your indoor +2/3 saved shadow detail, and the sunset best frame was -1/3. You update your mental model for next time.

Reflection Question

How would you change your bracketing strategy if film and processing were free, and how would you change it if you had only six frames left—what does that reveal about your true exposure priorities?

MICROECONOMICS
--------------

Topic

Preferences and Budget Constraints

Lesson

From first principles: scarce resources and choices
Every consumer faces limited income and many wants. A budget constraint captures what bundles you can afford; preferences capture what bundles you like. Optimal choice is where what you can do meets what you want.

Budget constraints and opportunity sets
Suppose there are two goods, X and Y, with prices pX and pY, and income I. The budget constraint is pX·X + pY·Y ≤ I. The opportunity set is all bundles (X, Y) that satisfy it. The budget line is where you spend everything: pX·X + pY·Y = I.
- Intercepts: If you buy only X, you get I/pX units. If only Y, I/pY units.
- Slope: Rearranging, Y = (I/pY) − (pX/pY)·X. The slope is −(pX/pY), the rate at which the market lets you trade X for Y. It is the relative price.
A price change pivots the line; an income change shifts it in or out without changing slope (unless prices change too).

Preferences: properties we assume
To reason cleanly, we assume preferences are:
- Complete: For any two bundles A and B, you can say A is at least as good as B, or B is at least as good as A (or both).
- Transitive: If A is at least as good as B, and B as C, then A is at least as good as C. This avoids cycles.
Often we also assume:
- More is better (monotonicity): Within reason, having more of each good does not hurt.
- Convexity (taste for variety): A mix of two bundles is at least as good as an extreme. This encodes diminishing marginal willingness to substitute.

Indifference curves and MRS
Preferences can be drawn as indifference curves: sets of bundles equally liked. They slope down (to keep utility constant, having more X requires giving up Y) and do not cross (that would violate transitivity). Convex curves bow toward the origin.
The marginal rate of substitution (MRS) at a point is how much Y you are willing to give up for one more unit of X while staying just as satisfied. It is the absolute slope of the indifference curve there. With well-behaved preferences, MRS falls as you get more X (diminishing MRS).

Choosing the best bundle
Your best affordable bundle sits where an indifference curve is just tangent to the budget line—if the solution is interior. At that point, your personal trade-off equals the market trade-off:
MRS = pX/pY.
- If at your current point MRS > pX/pY, you value X on the margin more than its price implies; you should buy more X.
- If MRS < pX/pY, you should buy more Y.
Corner solutions occur when the best point is at an intercept: you specialize in one good. This happens if your MRS never equals the price ratio in the feasible set or if goods are perfect substitutes or strong preferences push you to an edge.

Interpreting changes
- Income increase: Parallel outward shift. You can afford more of both. For normal goods, you buy more; for inferior goods, you might buy less of the inferior good even as income rises.
- Price change: The line pivots. A fall in pX makes X relatively cheaper. The new best point compares your new MRS at tangency. Substitution effect (toward the cheaper good) and income effect (change in real purchasing power) both play roles, but at this stage the geometry is enough: you end at a new tangency or corner.

Limits of the framework
Real people face indivisible goods, uncertainty, and mental accounting. Still, the budget-preference picture gives a clear first approximation: choices balance a internal valuation (MRS) against external prices (pX/pY) under a hard constraint (I).

Mental model: trade-offs on a slope
See the slope as a price story and the curve as a taste story. Equality of slopes is agreement between you and the market at the margin. If they disagree, re-balance until they match or until you hit a boundary.

Example

You have $100. Coffees cost $5 (X), bagels cost $2 (Y). The budget line is 5X + 2Y = 100. Intercepts: 20 coffees or 50 bagels. Slope: −(5/2) = −2.5; the market trades 1 coffee for 2.5 bagels. If, at 10 coffees and 25 bagels, your personal MRS is 4 bagels per coffee (you’d give up 4 bagels for 1 more coffee), you value coffee more than its relative price. You should move toward more coffee and fewer bagels until your MRS falls to 2.5. If instead your MRS were 1, you would shift toward more bagels until your MRS rose to 2.5 or you hit the bagel-only corner.

Scenario

A student has $60/month for digital services. A data plan top-up (X) costs $15; a streaming channel (Y) costs $10. Line: 15X + 10Y = 60. Intercepts: 4 top-ups or 6 channels. If a campus Wi‑Fi upgrade makes data less valuable, her MRS of data for streaming falls below 1.5 (the price ratio 15/10). She drops one top-up and adds a channel, moving along the line to a new tangency. Later, a promo drops streaming to $8; the budget line pivots out for Y, and she substitutes further toward streaming, unless she hits a practical corner (there are only so many channels she actually wants).

Reflection Question

When would a corner solution—buying only one of two goods—make rational sense for you, and what does that reveal about your marginal rate of substitution in that situation?

MICROSOFT 365 ADMINISTRATOR (MS-102)
------------------------------------

Topic

Tenant provisioning and baseline configuration

Lesson

First principles: identity first, secure by default
A Microsoft 365 tenant is your organization’s identity and data boundary. Everything—email, files, devices—hangs from this root. Provisioning is not just signing up; it sets the security posture that will echo through every mailbox and team. Your guiding ideas: identity is the new perimeter, least privilege, and secure by default.

Create the tenant
1) Choose region and tenant name. The region determines data residency. The initial domain looks like yourname.onmicrosoft.com. Pick a name you can live with; it cannot be changed.
2) Create the first admin. Use a strong passphrase, and plan a second, separate break-glass account with a long, unique password stored offline. Do not enable MFA on the break-glass account, but do restrict its use and monitor it.
3) Licenses. Start a trial or apply paid licenses. Know what features exist only with certain SKUs (e.g., advanced security, device management). Assign no licenses to the break-glass account.

Add and verify your custom domain
- In Microsoft 365 admin center, add yourdomain.com and follow DNS verification. Publish the TXT record at your registrar. After verification, add MX, CNAME, and SPF records when you are ready to route mail.
- Do not change MX until you are prepared to receive mail in Microsoft 365.

Baseline security configuration
If you are small or starting fresh, enabling Security Defaults is the fastest win. It enforces MFA for admins, blocks legacy authentication, and requires modern auth. If you need fine control, use Conditional Access instead of Security Defaults. Key baselines:
- MFA for admins and privileged roles. Require phishing-resistant methods where available. Exclude only the break-glass account.
- Disable legacy protocols. Block basic auth for protocols like POP/IMAP unless you have a documented exception.
- Create role groups with least privilege. Do not use Global Administrator for daily work. Assign specific roles (Exchange Admin, SharePoint Admin, etc.) only when needed.
- Audit and logging. Ensure unified audit logging is on. Set alerting for risky sign-ins and admin role changes.
- User lifecycle. Decide how users are created (cloud-only or synchronized from on-prem AD). Turn on self-service password reset with appropriate options and registration enforcement.

Baseline privacy and org-wide settings
- Organization profile: set technical and security contacts. Add privacy statement and branding if appropriate.
- External collaboration defaults: in Entra ID and Microsoft 365 groups/SharePoint, set who can invite guests and what guests can do. Start conservative, then loosen with business justification.
- Mail hygiene: configure anti-malware and anti-phishing presets. Start with a standard preset policy for all recipients; increase to strict for high-risk users (admins, finance). Publish SPF for your domain; plan DKIM and DMARC once mail is stable.
- SharePoint/OneDrive sharing: begin with new and existing guests, block anonymous links unless required. Set sensible default link permissions (view, not edit).

Device and app baseline
- Enable device management (Intune) if licensed. Create a basic compliance policy (require PIN/biometric, encryption) and app protection for mobile. Block unmanaged devices from accessing data, or at least restrict to web-only until devices are enrolled.
- Conditional Access starter policies: require MFA for admins, require compliant or hybrid-joined devices for sensitive apps, block legacy auth, and require MFA for risky sign-ins.

First-week checklist mindset
- Protect the top: secure admin accounts first.
- Protect the edge: block legacy, enforce MFA.
- Protect the data: strong sharing defaults, mail hygiene, and logging.
- Plan exceptions: document and track any deviations with owners and review dates.

Documentation and recovery
- Store break-glass credentials offline and test that you can sign in if CA policies misfire. Monitor that account’s inactivity and sign-in alerts.
- Document DNS changes, user creation flows, and where logs and alerts live. Small notes now prevent big outages later.

Secure by default
Favor defaults and presets that raise the floor for everyone. Customize only when you have a clear need. Simplicity reduces misconfiguration risk and speeds recovery when something breaks.

Example

A 20-person firm signs up for Microsoft 365 Business Premium. They name the tenant acme-co and create two admin accounts: admin-jane for daily tasks with MFA, and breakglass-acme with a 24-character password sealed in a safe, used only for emergencies. They verify acme.com with a TXT record but wait to switch MX until a weekend cutover. They enable Security Defaults, then later move to Conditional Access to exclude a legacy scanner account from IMAP while they plan a replacement. They set SharePoint sharing to new and existing guests and default links to view. In Exchange, they apply the standard preset security policy and add finance and executives to the stricter policy. Unified audit logging is confirmed on, and SSPR is enabled for all users with registration enforced.

Scenario

You are asked to build a test tenant to evaluate Teams. You create a trial in the correct region, add test users, and enable Security Defaults. Before piloting, you add your lab domain and publish DKIM after mail is steady. You configure a Conditional Access rule requiring MFA for admins but exclude your break-glass account and store its password offline. During testing, a policy blocks your device; using the break-glass account, you sign in, review sign-in logs, and fix the misconfigured condition. The pilot resumes with minimal downtime.

Reflection Question

Where would you deliberately accept less convenience or flexibility on day one in exchange for a stronger default security posture, and how would you explain that trade-off to non-technical leaders?

WISDOM
------

Topic

Reasoning Basics

Lesson

Thinking from first principles
Reasoning is about moving from what you know to what you should believe or do. Two core engines drive this movement: deduction and induction. A third, often practical one, is abduction. Understanding what each can and cannot guarantee keeps your mind honest.

Deduction: truth-preserving structure
In deduction, if the premises are true and the form is valid, the conclusion must be true. It preserves truth.
- Example form: All A are B. C is A. Therefore, C is B. If the form is valid, the only way the conclusion fails is if a premise is false.
- Strengths: Certainty when premises are solid. Great for math, contracts, and definitions.
- Limits: It adds no new factual content beyond what was already contained implicitly in the premises. If your premises are shaky, deduction propagates error flawlessly.

Induction: pattern-seeking under uncertainty
Induction generalizes from observations to broader claims (or predicts the next case based on the past).
- Example: The sun has risen every day of my life; it will rise tomorrow. The structure is not truth-preserving; it is probability-raising.
- Strengths: It learns from experience; it is how science moves from data to models.
- Limits: Susceptible to hidden variables, sampling bias, and black swans. It requires humility: update beliefs when counterexamples or better data arrive.

Abduction: best explanation
Abduction infers the most plausible explanation for surprising facts.
- Example: The ground is wet; the best explanation is that it rained. Alternative explanations exist (sprinklers, a spill). Abduction weighs simplicity and fit.
- Strengths: Drives hypothesis generation and everyday diagnosis.
- Limits: "Best" can be subjective; priors matter.

Common fallacies to avoid
- Straw man: Attacking a weaker version of someone’s claim.
- Ad hominem: Attacking the person rather than the argument.
- Post hoc: Assuming that because B followed A, A caused B.
- Appeal to authority: Treating a claim as true solely because an authority said it, outside their expertise or without evidence.
- False dilemma: Presenting two options as the only options.
- Confirmation bias (cognitive, but it fuels fallacies): Seeking evidence that supports your view and ignoring disconfirming data.

A practical logic tree
When facing a decision or claim, build a quick logic tree:
1) Define the claim or decision clearly.
2) List reasons for and against (branches).
3) Attach evidence to each reason; note its quality (direct data, expert consensus, anecdote).
4) Surface assumptions for each branch; label the riskiest ones.
5) Test high-risk assumptions with a small experiment or by seeking a disconfirming case.
This blends deduction (consistency), induction (data), and abduction (explanations) into a single workflow.

Signals of sound reasoning
- Clear premises separated from conclusions.
- Willingness to state uncertainty and conditions.
- Openness to counterexamples.
- Proportionate confidence: strong claims rest on strong, varied evidence.

Mental habits that help
- Steelman before you critique: restate the strongest version of an opposing view. It protects you from straw men.
- Numerical sanity checks: when possible, put rough numbers to claims. Basic magnitudes catch nonsense.
- Time-stamp beliefs: note when and why you believed something. When the world changes, you notice.

Putting it together
Use deduction to check internal coherence, induction to learn from the world, and abduction to generate and compare explanations. Avoid common traps by naming them. When stakes are high, slow down and build a logic tree; when stakes are low, a quick mental check for fallacies and a sanity check often suffice.

Example

Claim: A new policy increased sales. Post hoc trap: sales rose after the policy, but also after a holiday ad campaign. Build a logic tree: define the claim; collect data before/after policy while controlling for ads and seasonality; compare to regions without the policy (a quasi-experiment). Deductively, the argument needs a causal link; inductively, the difference-in-differences data strengthen or weaken the claim; abductively, the best explanation might be the ad campaign, not the policy.

Scenario

Your team debates launching a feature. Deduction: the specs imply compatibility with current APIs; if the specs are accurate, integration should work. Induction: past launches with similar scope slipped by two weeks; plan buffers. Abduction: the most plausible reason a competitor added a similar feature is churn pressure, not pure innovation; adjust expectations. You steelman the skeptical view, list assumptions that would break the launch, and test the riskiest one with a one-week prototype.

Reflection Question

When facing a complex choice, which part of your reasoning do you tend to overtrust—your deductions from neat premises, your pattern-matching from experience, or your favored explanation—and how can you balance the three?

FICTION-WRITING
---------------

Topic

Beginnings and Hooks

Lesson

First principles: promise and tension
An opening earns the reader’s next page by making a clear promise and creating tension. Promise says what kind of experience this story will deliver. Tension is an imbalance: a want unmet, a danger uncontained, a secret unexplained. Without promise, readers feel lost; without tension, they feel bored.

The Hook Triangle: character, context, question
- Character: Who are we with, and why should we care? Empathy comes from specificity and vulnerability more than likability.
- Context: Where and when are we, and what rules seem to govern this world? A single concrete detail can anchor pages of ambiguity.
- Question: What unresolved problem, desire, or mystery pulls us forward? The question should be answerable by reading on, not by leaving the book.
A strong opening hits all three quickly: a person in a place facing a problem.

Reader Empathy Loop
Readers invest when they sense a human interior. Three signals build this fast:
- Specificity: precise, sensory details (not many, but sharp).
- Vulnerability: a crack that reveals what matters to the character.
- Motion: a small decision or action that shows agency.
This loop creates curiosity about how the interior meets the world.

Tension is not noise
Action is not the same as tension. Tension comes from stakes and uncertainty. A quiet scene with a forbidden text message can be more tense than a car chase if the consequences are clearer and the outcome uncertain.

Techniques for strong openings
- Start late: enter as close to the turn as possible. Cut setup that readers can infer.
- Plant a concrete anchor: one vivid object or sensory cue to ground the scene.
- Imply backstory, don’t unload it: let context leak through word choice, status dynamics, or a telling gap.
- Make a micro-promise: a line that hints at the shape of the story (tone, scope, genre).
- Sharpen verbs, prune qualifiers: line-level energy matters; openings carry weight.
- Name the want: if the protagonist wants something on page one, readers lean in.

Diagnosing weak openings
- Throat-clearing: weather reports, waking up, mirror descriptions, world history lectures.
- Floaty vagueness: no names, no place, no sensory anchor.
- Overpopulation: too many names before readers latch onto one.
- Question without context: mysteries that feel like withholding rather than intrigue.
- Info-dump: backstory blocks that interrupt motion.

A revision pass that works
1) Cut the first paragraph. Most drafts start too early. See if the story still makes sense.
2) Surface the want. Add a line of internal or external action that makes the desire concrete.
3) Anchor the place with one sensory detail that also carries mood.
4) Raise a specific question in the reader’s mind, ideally about consequence to the character.
5) Swap two bland verbs for sharper ones; remove two filler words per sentence.
6) Check the first 150 words for the Hook Triangle. If one side is weak, fix that side—don’t stuff more words in; trade general for specific.

Balancing clarity and mystery
Give enough clarity that readers can track who/where/what, then add mystery about why/how. If readers cannot answer the first set, they cannot care about the second.

Voice and genre promise
Openings also tune expectation. A sardonic first line signals different promises than a lyrical one. Align tone with genre: a thriller can open with an implied clock; a literary story can open with an inner dissonance—but both still need character, context, and question.

Example

By the time the train slid into Graymouth, Mara had rehearsed the apology nine times and none of them survived past the word sorry. She kept her sister’s last voicemail open like a talisman, screen dimmed to a single blinking dot. The platform smelled of rain and hot metal. If she turned left, the hospital. If she turned right, the bar where it all started. The dot blinked again. She turned left. (Character: Mara, vulnerable and active. Context: a specific station with sensory anchors. Question: What did she do, and will the apology work?)

Scenario

You are submitting to an agent who reads the first five pages. Your draft opens with a two-page prologue of ancient lore. You cut it and begin with your protagonist stealing a key in a museum at night. You add one grounded detail—the guard’s thermos sweating on the desk—and a line that reveals the want: if she doesn’t steal the key, her brother loses the house. The reader now meets a person in a place with a clear problem and consequence, aligned with the heist-thriller promise.

Reflection Question

In your current work-in-progress, what single, specific change to the opening would most increase reader tension without adding any new backstory?
