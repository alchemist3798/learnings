FILM-PHOTOGRAPHY
----------------

Topic

Intro to Flash with Film

Lesson

Why use flash with film
Flash is a way to place light exactly where you want it. It freezes motion, lifts shadows, balances harsh sun, and creates consistency when ambient light is unreliable. With film, you cannot chimp a histogram, so you lean on first principles and a simple workflow.

First principles: light and time
A flash is a small burst of energy. Most small flashes fire for a few thousandths of a second. Because the burst is so short, aperture mainly controls flash exposure, while shutter speed (up to the sync limit) mainly controls ambient exposure. Two core ideas guide everything:
- Inverse square law: Light falls off with the square of distance. Double the distance, and you get one quarter the light, about two stops dimmer.
- Energy budget: Your flash has finite energy per pop. You spend that energy on distance and on modifiers like bounce and diffusion. Bigger, farther, and softer costs power.

Guide numbers: the fast way to set exposure
A guide number (GN) tells you how powerful a flash is at ISO 100. The simple formula is GN = distance × f-number. Rearranged, f-number = GN ÷ distance. If your flash GN is 36 meters at ISO 100 and your subject is 3 meters away, f-number is 36 ÷ 3 = f11 to f12, so set f11.

Adjusting for ISO and power
ISO changes effective GN by the square root of the ISO ratio. Effective GN = GN100 × sqrt(ISO ÷ 100). Going from ISO 100 to ISO 400 doubles GN. Manual power changes GN by the square root of the power fraction: half power reduces GN by about 0.71, quarter power halves GN, and so on. Think in stops: one stop less power drops GN by about 0.71, two stops less halves GN.

Sync shutters safely
Focal-plane shutters expose using two curtains. At or below the X-sync speed, the whole frame is open when the flash fires. Above it, you get a black band because part of the film is covered. Typical sync speeds are 1/60 to 1/250. Leaf shutters open fully and can sync at any speed the shutter offers. Stay at or below your camera’s sync speed unless you know a special mode and flash that support high-speed sync. Most film bodies and manual flashes do not, and HSS drastically reduces effective power.

Avoid high-voltage triggers
Many old flashes have high trigger voltages that can damage modern hot shoes or radio triggers. If in doubt, do not mount it directly. Use a safe radio trigger, an optical slave with isolation, or a dedicated flash rated as safe. If you measure with a multimeter, be aware you are still taking a risk; better to look up your exact model from a trusted source and use an isolator if uncertain. PC sync ports are delicate; route cables carefully.

Bounce and diffuse for softer light
Hard light comes from a small apparent source. Make the source larger, and shadows soften. Bouncing off a wall or ceiling increases the apparent size and spreads light. Diffusers do similar work by enlarging the emitting surface. The trade-off is power. Expect bounce to cost roughly 2 to 3 stops, and diffusion 1 to 2 stops, depending on distance and material. Choose clean, neutral surfaces; ceilings should be reasonably low and white. Angle matters: a 45 up and 45 back aim often gives pleasing, directional top-front light.

Balancing flash with ambient
Decide the scene you want. Meter ambient for the background and pick a shutter speed at or below sync. Aperture will then set subject brightness from the flash. To do fill flash outdoors, set your ambient exposure first, then set flash one to two stops under that exposure at the subject distance, either by power or distance. Shutter speed adjusts background brightness without much changing the flash-lit subject, as long as you remain below sync.

Color and consistency
Match flash color to ambient when needed. Tungsten rooms may look orange; add a CTO gel on the flash and use tungsten-balanced film or a blue filter on the lens. Account for the gel cost in power, often about one stop for full CTO.

A quick workflow
- Set film ISO on your meter.
- Pick the ambient look: choose shutter under sync and meter to taste.
- Compute aperture from GN and distance, adjusted for ISO and modifiers.
- Choose manual flash power to hit that aperture. If power is insufficient, move closer, raise power, open aperture, or reduce modifiers.
- For negative film, consider slight overexposure of ambient and small bracketing of flash power for safety.
- Watch for reflections, raccoon eyes, and color casts.

These basics, plus a patient test roll, build intuition fast. You will soon be able to estimate by eye and confirm with simple math.

Example

You have ISO 400 film and a flash with GN 36 meters at ISO 100. Subject is 3 meters away. First, scale GN for ISO 400: 36 × sqrt(400 ÷ 100) = 36 × 2 = 72. Direct flash would suggest f-number 72 ÷ 3 = f24, so use f22. If you bounce off a white ceiling and estimate a 2-stop loss, halve GN to 36, giving 36 ÷ 3 = f12, so choose f11. If you want one stop less flash for gentle fill, drop power one stop from whatever got you f11.

Scenario

You are photographing a couple on 400-speed color negative film at a dim reception. Ceiling is white at about 3 meters. You set shutter to 1/60 to keep some ambient glow. Your flash is GN 36 at ISO 100. At ISO 400, effective GN is 72, but bounce costs an estimated 2 stops, back to GN 36. The couple is 2.5 meters away along the bounce path you visualize. Compute 36 ÷ 2.5 ≈ f14, choose f13 or f11 for a touch more ambient. You angle the head 45 up and behind, add a small bounce card for catchlights, and set power to match. Test, then slightly nudge power down for softer fill as dancing starts.

Reflection Question

When you cannot get enough power for your chosen look, how do you decide which knob to turn first: distance, aperture, ISO, modifiers, or composition, and why?

MICROECONOMICS
--------------

Topic

Marginal Utility and Diminishing Returns

Lesson

Start from choices under scarcity
Every day you allocate limited time and money among many options. Utility is a simple way to describe your preferences. It is not about moral worth or exact units; it just orders outcomes from less preferred to more preferred. The key tool is marginal analysis: focus on the next unit, not the average so far.

Marginal utility
Marginal utility is the additional satisfaction from one more unit of a good or activity, holding everything else the same. The first coffee in the morning might feel amazing. The fourth feels less special. What matters for a decision is whether the next coffee is worth it compared to your next best alternative.

Diminishing marginal utility
For most goods, as you consume more in a short period, additional units add less extra satisfaction. This is diminishing marginal utility. It does not say total utility falls; total may still rise, just more slowly. Nor does it say every next unit is worse for all goods in all ranges. It says the typical pattern is tapering marginal gains because needs get satisfied and variety matters.

Equal marginal utility per dollar rule
When you have a budget and two or more goods, total utility is maximized when the last dollar spent on each good yields the same marginal utility per dollar. Written as a rule of thumb: marginal utility of X divided by price of X equals marginal utility of Y divided by price of Y. If the ratios are not equal, shifting one dollar from the lower ratio to the higher ratio increases total utility.

Why the rule makes sense
Imagine you are at a buffet of choices for your budget. If one option gives more extra satisfaction per dollar than another, moving a dollar into the better option increases your total. You keep shuffling dollars until no dollar does better elsewhere. This is a stop rule rather than a perfect calculation; you rarely know exact numbers, but the direction is clear.

What happens when prices change
Suppose the price of X falls. Immediately, marginal utility per dollar of X rises because the denominator fell. You will want more X until its marginal utility falls enough, due to diminishing marginal utility, to restore equality with Y. This blend of substitution toward the cheaper option and an effective income boost from lower prices underlies the law of demand. The details depend on how quickly marginal utility declines and on whether goods are complements or substitutes.

What happens when income changes
With more income, you move to higher bundles along your preferences. You add units where marginal utility per dollar is currently highest and equalize again at a new level. Normal goods rise with income. Some goods are inferior; their marginal utility may fall fast, or other goods now offer more satisfaction per dollar, so you buy less of the inferior good even with more income.

Avoid common traps
- Focus on the next unit. Sunk costs and averages are irrelevant to the next choice.
- Watch for satiation. At some point the next unit adds near zero utility; stop and reallocate.
- Consider time. Marginal utility today can differ from tomorrow because of habits, health, or variety seeking.
- Beware framing. Marketing can inflate perceived marginal utility; compare alternatives deliberately.

A simple method you can use
- Make a short list of options that compete for the same budget or time.
- Estimate the next-unit benefit for each and divide by price or time cost. Use rough scores if needed.
- Move your next dollar or minute to the option with the highest ratio.
- Re-estimate when a choice changes your context, and stop when the ratios are close.

Limits and judgment
The utility model is a tool, not a life rule. Social ties, fairness, identity, and long-term goals can dominate short-run marginal pleasure. Still, marginal thinking is powerful for routine tradeoffs because it keeps you from overspending on the first good that comes to mind and neglecting the second best that might now deliver more satisfaction per dollar.

Example

You have 10 dollars and want caffeine and snacks. Coffee is 2 dollars; the first, second, third cups give marginal utility scores of 10, 6, and 3. Bagels are 2 dollars; the first, second, third give 8, 5, and 2. Compute MU per dollar. First dollar to coffee: 10 ÷ 2 = 5. Next best is bagel: 8 ÷ 2 = 4. Spend dollars in order of highest MU per dollar: coffee (5), bagel (4), coffee again (6 ÷ 2 = 3), bagel again (5 ÷ 2 = 2.5), then decide if the third coffee at 1.5 beats the third bagel at 1. You stop when another dollar would do better elsewhere.

Scenario

A grocery store drops the price of strawberries from 6 to 3 while blueberries stay at 3. You like both but usually split your fruit budget. With strawberries cheaper, their marginal utility per dollar jumps. You shift more budget to strawberries until the extra satisfaction from yet another box falls enough that blueberries again tie on marginal utility per dollar. You still buy some blueberries if the variety itself keeps their next-unit benefit from collapsing.

Reflection Question

When a price cut tempts you to buy more of one thing, how do you decide the point where another dollar back into variety or a different category would bring you more satisfaction overall?

MICROSOFT 365 ADMINISTRATOR (MS-102)
------------------------------------

Topic

Organization profile and company branding

Lesson

Purpose
Organization profile and company branding shape how users and guests first encounter your tenant. Good setup builds trust, reduces phishing risk, and smooths sign in. Think user experience first: clear identity, fast load, accessible design, and accurate contact details.

Organization profile fundamentals
In the Microsoft 365 admin center, go to Settings, Org settings, then Organization profile. Fill out:
- Organization name and legal information. Ensure it matches public records where practical.
- Address, region, and preferred language. This informs service behavior and communications.
- Technical, security, and privacy contacts. Use monitored group mailboxes rather than individuals so messages are not lost.
- Support information. Provide a help desk email, phone, and portal link that users recognize.
- Release preferences. Choose targeted release only for ringed pilot groups; document who is in those rings.
- Default domain. Add and verify your vanity domain so user principal names and email addresses align with your brand.
Keep the profile synchronized with HR and legal, and review it during major org changes.

Company branding in Microsoft Entra ID
Company branding customizes Microsoft-hosted sign-in pages across web and native apps. In the Microsoft Entra admin center, go to Identity, Users, Company branding. Create a default branding and, if needed, locale-specific versions. Typical elements include:
- Banner logo, often 280x60 pixels, transparent PNG.
- Square logo, about 240x240 pixels, used in some tiles and dark mode contexts.
- Background image, commonly 1920x1080, compressed to keep size small.
- Background color and accent color that meet contrast guidelines.
- Sign-in page text, such as a friendly welcome and support link.
- Footer links to privacy and acceptable use pages under your domain.
Feature availability can depend on your subscription. Apply branding carefully and test thoroughly.

Design for trust and speed
- Keep images lightweight. Large images slow sign in. Target a few hundred kilobytes or less per image.
- Ensure accessibility. Choose high-contrast colors and add alt text for logos. Test with dark mode.
- Avoid stock imagery that resembles phishing lures. Use a simple brand mark and neutral background.
- Use your verified domain in help links so users can validate authenticity.

Locale and device considerations
You can provide different branding per language. Prioritize locales with many users. Test on mobile, desktop, and varying network speeds. Check both web sign in and device enrollment or Office app dialogs, which may display elements differently.

Testing and rollout
- Stage in a nonproduction tenant or apply to a pilot group if available.
- Test with private browsing to avoid cached resources.
- Verify flows: user sign in, guest invitation redemption, password reset, and self-service sign up if enabled.
- Confirm that help links, logo alt text, and privacy links work.
- Validate that branding does not obscure system messages or captcha challenges.
Document what changed, when, and who approved. Keep source files and a rollback set.

Security posture
Branding deters some phishing by giving users familiar cues, but it is not a control by itself. Train users to check your domain, use passwordless or MFA, and report suspicious prompts. Do not include sensitive details on the sign-in page. Keep contact info generic but useful.

Governance checklist
- Ownership: assign a team for profile and branding maintenance.
- Change cadence: review quarterly or during rebrands.
- Compliance: ensure legal texts and privacy links are current.
- Monitoring: after changes, watch help desk tickets for confusion or sign-in issues.
With a thoughtful setup, users sign in with less friction, and your tenant presents a coherent, trustworthy face to the world.

Example

You prepare assets: a 280x60 transparent logo, a 240x240 square mark, and a 1920x1080 background with subtle texture compressed under 250 KB. In Microsoft Entra admin center, you create default company branding, set the background color to match your brand, add a short welcome line and a help link under your domain. You test in a private browser on desktop and on a phone. The contrast is good, load time is fast, and the help link works.

Scenario

Your company merges and updates its name. You update the Microsoft 365 Organization profile with the new legal name, address, and support contact. You add the new domain, verify DNS, and set it as default so new users get the right UPN. In Microsoft Entra, you replace the banner and background with the new brand, add a note to direct legacy users to a merger FAQ, and localize the text for two languages. You announce the change to staff and confirm that guest invitations show the new branding without broken links.

Reflection Question

If a user were seeing your sign-in page for the first time, what small design or content choice would most increase their confidence that this page is authentic and safe, and why?

WISDOM
------

Topic

Falsification and Testing

Lesson

Start with the aim
If the aim is to get closer to truth, then beliefs must face real risk. A claim that cannot be wronged by reality is not informative. Falsification means you design tests that a wrong idea would likely fail and a right idea would likely pass. You stop asking only what would make you feel more certain and start asking what could show you are mistaken.

Make claims precise enough to fail
A vague belief can dodge any outcome. Shape it into a prediction about what, where, and when. Replace rain will be less frequent with In the next 90 days, this location will have fewer than five days with more than 5 millimeters of rain, measured by this station. Now there is a clear scoreboard. Precision forces courage.

Set thresholds and stop rules before you look
Define what result would count as a failure and what would count as adequate support. Decide the sample size or time window now, not later. This blocks the temptation to chase significance by slicing the data until it sings. Preregistration is just a public version of this habit; a private notebook can serve the same discipline.

Seek contrary evidence first
The easiest way to be wrong for a long time is to avoid the disconfirming case. Ask What observation would most challenge this idea. Design the test so that such observations have a fair chance to show up. A good test exposes the claim to its natural predators.

Steelman the alternative
Before testing, build the strongest rival explanation you can. What would a smart critic say. What pattern would the rival predict that yours would not. Now your test becomes a duel between competing predictions. This is more severe and more informative than a test that only your view could pass.

Use controls, baselines, and blinding
Comparisons rescue you from noise. A control group, a baseline period, or a placebo helps you see what would have happened anyway. Blinding removes cues that leak expectations into outcomes. Even in daily life, you can blind yourself. For example, hide brand labels in taste tests; compare two workflows without knowing which is which until after you rate them.

Think in terms of update, not proof
One test rarely proves anything. It shifts how much credence you give a claim. Large, clean, risky tests move your belief a lot. Small, messy, easy tests barely move it. Track the direction and size of your updates. If you never update downward, your pipeline is biased.

Design for cost of error
False positives and false negatives have different costs. If a safety claim fails, you want high power to detect the problem. If you test an expensive intervention, you also want to protect against false hope. Set severity in proportion to the stakes, and consider staged testing that gets harsher as you invest more.

Record predictions and look back
A prediction log is humbling and invaluable. Write the claim, the test, the threshold, and the result. Later, audit yourself. Where did you move the goalposts. Where were you overconfident. Over time you learn which heuristics serve you and where your blind spots live.

Culture and emotion
It is hard to invite disproof when identity rides on a belief. Create psychological slack. Treat a failed prediction as a fee for tuition rather than a bruise to ego. Favor environments where people can change their mind without losing face. Truth grows faster where dissent is safe.

In short, falsification is not cynicism; it is disciplined curiosity. By asking for the strongest tests you can stand, you trade fragile confidence for durable understanding.

Example

You believe a new morning routine boosts focus. Instead of tracking vague vibes, you define a test: for two weeks you alternate routine days and control days. Each day, before knowing which it is, you do a 10-minute attention task and log score and time to first distraction. You set a threshold in advance: an average 10 percent improvement on both metrics with a 95 percent confidence interval not overlapping zero. At the end, you compare blinded labels and decide based on the pre-set rule.

Scenario

Your team thinks shorter meetings will raise project throughput. You identify two similar squads and flip a coin to assign one to 15-minute standups and the other to 30-minute standups for a month. You define primary outcomes now: cycle time and blocked tasks per week, and a list of potential confounders to monitor. You commit to a minimum of four weeks and to publishing the result internally either way. If the difference is small or favors longer standups, you keep the old format or try a new design with a harsher test.

Reflection Question

Which cherished belief of yours could survive a severe, clearly defined test, and what specific observation would genuinely make you say I was wrong?

FICTION-WRITING
---------------

Topic

Three-Act Structure Overview

Lesson

What three acts really are
Three acts are not a formula so much as a way to organize change: beginning, middle, end. Act I sets up a world, a character, and a problem that cannot be ignored. Act II explores resistance and transformation as efforts escalate and fail until something crucial is learned. Act III resolves the main question through a decisive confrontation and its consequences. The hinge points are made of character choices, not just events that happen to them.

Act I: setup and the first doorway
- Establish normal life, stakes, and tone quickly. Show what is missing for the protagonist internally and externally.
- Inciting incident disturbs the status quo. It may be an invitation, a threat, or a discovery that demands attention.
- First plot point, or doorway to Act II, is where the protagonist chooses to engage. It is a no way back turn; the story promise becomes specific. Place a clear decision here.

Act II: rising action, midpoint shift, and crisis
- First half of Act II is reaction. The protagonist tries obvious strategies and meets increasingly strong opposition. Allies, antagonists, and subplots complicate the path.
- Midpoint is a revelation, victory with a cost, or a mirror moment that changes understanding. After it, the protagonist becomes more proactive or the stakes reframe.
- Second half of Act II is attack. Plans are bolder but provoke backlash. Try-fail cycles tighten. End with an all is lost beat and a dark night of the soul, where the old self cannot carry on.

Act III: climax and resolution
- The Act II to III turn is a choice to act from the new understanding. The protagonist commits to a risky plan that expresses internal change.
- The climax is the decisive confrontation in which the central question is answered. Make it a test of will and values, not just logistics.
- Resolution shows the new normal. Pay off setups, show the cost, and leave a final image that echoes your theme.

Placing character decisions at hinges
At the end of Act I, a choice to step through the doorway. At the midpoint, a choice about meaning or strategy. At the end of Act II, a recommitment after loss. In Act III, a choice under maximum pressure. If these are passive, the structure feels hollow; if they are active, even small plots feel inevitable.

A quick outlining method for a short story
- Premise in one sentence: a person with a need faces an obstacle and must change to get what they want.
- External goal and internal need: what they want vs what they lack inside.
- Act I: sketch the normal, the inciting incident, and the doorway choice. Aim for 20 to 30 percent of length.
- Act II: list three escalating attempts, a midpoint shift, and how allies or antagonists complicate things. Aim for 50 to 60 percent.
- Act III: define the irreversible act, the climax beat, and two or three payoff images. Aim for 20 to 30 percent.
- Sanity check: are the act breaks anchored by decisions, are stakes clear, and do setups pay off.

Flexibility and voice
Percentages are guidelines. Literary work may compress or expand acts. The bones still help you place pressure and meaning. Use Save the Cat style beats if they help you plan moments, but keep your eye on the throughline of change. Structure serves emotion, not the other way around.

In the end, three acts give readers a subconscious rhythm: commitment, struggle, transformation. If you place decisions at the hinges and escalate consequences, readers will feel the current pull them through.

Example

Outline a 3,000-word courier story. Premise: A bike courier must deliver a package across a city during a blackout and learns to ask for help. Act I 700 words: show harried routine; blackout hits; client offers double if on time; courier chooses to risk it and leaves the safe central district. Act II 1,600 words: streets clog; attempt 1 detour fails; midpoint reveal that the package is medical supplies for a child; courier asks a rival for directions; attempt 2 uses alleys but a tire blows; attempt 3 shortcuts through a park and a storm rolls in; all is lost when the bridge is closed. Dark night: admits fear of relying on others. Act III 700 words: chooses to enlist strangers to form a relay; climax is a sprint through a service tunnel; resolution shows the delivery made and the courier sharing credit, hinting at a new way of living.

Scenario

You join a writing group with a 2,500-word deadline. You pitch a mystery about a librarian who must find a rare book before the library closes forever. The group asks you to mark act breaks. You define Act I ending as her decision to break policy and search locked stacks. Midpoint: she discovers the book is miscataloged and tied to her mentor’s secret. End of Act II: her job offer elsewhere evaporates after a misstep, and she chooses to risk her reputation to expose the forger. Act III: showdown in the archive and a resolution where the community rallies to save the library.

Reflection Question

If you changed one character decision at an act break in your current story, which one would most transform the theme, and what ripple effects would follow through the later beats?
