FILM-PHOTOGRAPHY
----------------

Topic

Loading and Unloading 35mm and 120 Film

Lesson

First principles: what film needs
Film is light-sensitive. Your job is to move a ribbon (35mm) or a roll (120) through a light-tight path, keep it flat behind the lens, and protect it from heat, moisture, and fingerprints. If light reaches film before or after exposure, you get fogging (haze or bands). If film isn’t flat or tensioned, frames blur or overlap. Think of the camera as a sealed tunnel: open only in shade, close firmly, move film smoothly, and keep it clean.

How 35mm works
35mm comes in a metal cartridge. The film leader sticks out; sprocket holes along the edges engage camera teeth that pull the film. Inside the back of the camera is a pressure plate that keeps film flat. Loading is about three things: seat the cartridge, feed the leader into the take-up, and confirm the sprockets catch. Do it in the shade; sunlight bouncing into an open back can fog the first frames.

Loading 35mm (general flow)
- Shade the camera. Open the back.
- Place the cartridge in its chamber (usually left). Pull the leader across to the take-up spool (right).
- Insert the leader tip into the slot or hook on the take-up. Advance gently so you see the spool turn and the sprockets grab holes on both sides.
- Keep light pressure on the rewind crank; as you advance, the crank should turn—this confirms transport.
- Close the back. Advance and fire until the counter reads 1. If the rewind crank isn’t turning while you advance, reopen and reseat the leader.

Unloading 35mm
- When the counter nears the roll’s end, tension will increase. Stop forcing the advance.
- Press the rewind release (usually on the bottom). Rewind with the crank until you feel tension drop and hear the leader slip into the cartridge.
- Only then open the back—in shade if possible. Remove the cartridge, cap with a film can, and label it (film type, ISO, date, push/pull notes). Store cool and dry.

How 120 works
120 is wider and on a spool with paper backing. You provide the empty take-up spool in the camera. Loading is about alignment and tightness. The backing paper protects the film from light, but only when wound tightly.

Loading 120 (general flow)
- Shade the camera. Open the back. Put the fresh roll in the supply chamber and an empty spool in the take-up chamber.
- Pull the paper leader across and anchor it into the slot on the take-up spool. Wind forward a bit to confirm it catches and wraps evenly.
- Align the printed START arrows on the backing paper with the camera’s start marks (they differ by camera—check the mark in the film gate or body casting).
- Close the back. Wind until the counter indicates frame 1. Keep the roll tight; slack invites light leaks at the edges.

Unloading 120
- After the last frame, keep winding until the paper tail fully wraps the film. The roll will feel tight.
- Remove the roll. Immediately seal it with the gummed sticker or a small piece of tape. The goal is a snug cylinder.
- Move the now-empty spool to the take-up side for the next roll. Label exposed rolls and store cool and dry.

Error-proofing (poka‑yoke) for both formats
- One orientation rule: always load with the camera back resting on your palm and the lens pointing down. This keeps leaders from slipping out and blocks stray light.
- Feel-for-rotation check: lightly touch the rewind crank (35mm) or watch the take-up knob (120) as you wind; rotation confirms transport.
- Color bands: put a green elastic on unshot rolls and a red elastic on exposed ones. Label immediately—future you is forgetful.
- Practice with a dead roll at home until your hands know the moves.

Common pitfalls and quick fixes
- Sprockets not engaged (35mm): reopen in shade, reseat leader, advance two strokes watching both edges.
- Loose 120 roll: press and roll it gently on a table to snug it; if it’s been loose in bright light, expect edge fog on outer frames.
- Opened back by accident: close immediately. Advance two frames (35mm) or one frame (120) and note possible fog on those frames.
- Heat exposure: don’t leave film in a hot car. If you did, develop sooner and expect more grain or color shifts.

A simple checklist
- Shade • Clean, dry hands • Seat cartridge/spools • Leader anchored • Transport turning • Close back • Wind to 1 • Label when done • Store cool.

Handle calm and slow. Film rewards smooth, repeatable moves more than speed.

Example

Memorable example: The belt-loop trick
Before loading, clip a small carabiner to your belt loop. Hang the camera by its strap on the carabiner so the lens points down and the back faces you. Your body makes shade, gravity keeps the leader seated, and both hands are free. Use this for both 35mm and 120: you can see the take-up action clearly and confirm transport without juggling the camera.

Scenario

You’re at a park at noon and want to switch from ISO 100 color to ISO 400 black-and-white. Trees cast dappled light. You step to the shaded side of a pavilion, hang the camera from your belt carabiner, and open the back. You load the 400, confirm the rewind crank turns as you advance, close, and wind to frame 1. A friend distracts you; before you walk back into sun, you press the rewind button on the old roll and stow it in a can with a red elastic, scribbling “CineStill 100, 6/28, frames 1–18” on tape. Later you notice a loose 120 roll in your bag. You immediately snug it by rolling it on a flat surface and seal it with tape, expecting mild edge fog on the first frame only.

Reflection Question

What small, repeatable move could you add to your loading routine that would prevent your most likely mistake from ever happening again?

MICROECONOMICS
--------------

Topic

Opportunity Cost and Trade-offs

Lesson

First principles
Scarcity forces choice. Every yes is also a no to something else. Opportunity cost is the value of the best alternative you give up when you choose. It’s not all the alternatives—just the most valuable next one. Because time, attention, energy, and money are limited, you want to spend them where the forgone best option is least painful relative to the gain.

Defining opportunity cost precisely
Opportunity cost = value of the best forgone alternative. If you take job A, the cost isn’t job B + C; it’s whichever of B or C you would have chosen next. If you spend two hours on Task X, the cost is the best thing you would have done with those same two hours, not whatever you might do some other day.

Units of value
Money is convenient, but not the only unit. Time, learning, relationships, health, and risk all matter. Convert to a common unit when possible. A simple method is to pick an anchor (often your hourly value) and translate benefits into that: cash in hand, expected future cash, skill growth discounted by probability, or happiness points if you’re introspecting.

Marginal thinking
Compare the next hour at the margin, not the whole project. If another hour on a report adds little value but the next hour on customer calls adds a lot, the marginal opportunity cost of staying on the report is high. Sunk costs don’t matter; only future choices do. Ask: “What is the best thing I can do with the next chunk of resources?”

Trade-offs across domains
- Money vs time: A cheaper option can be expensive if it burns time you could sell or enjoy.
- Focus vs variety: Specializing raises returns but increases risk; diversifying lowers variance but can dilute impact. Opportunity cost helps you choose balance.
- Now vs later: Immediate rewards can crowd out compounding gains (skills, relationships, health). Discount the future reasonably, but don’t ignore it.

Prioritizing with opportunity cost
1) List options you truly would do. 2) Estimate benefit, cost, and risk for each. 3) Compute net benefit per limiting resource (often per hour). 4) Rank by that ratio. 5) Select top choice; the runner-up defines your opportunity cost. 6) Re-evaluate when facts change.

Blind spots
- Ignoring your own time. Free tasks can be costly if they consume prime hours.
- Counting only cash. Learning, reputation, and joy are real value.
- Default bias. Doing “what’s on the calendar” has an opportunity cost too.
- Sunk-cost fallacy. Past effort is gone; choose based on tomorrow’s best options.
- Option value neglect. Keeping flexibility can be valuable if uncertainty is high.

Error-proofing with a checklist
- What is my scarce resource right now? (time, cash, energy, attention)
- What is the best alternative if I don’t do this?
- At the margin, which option has the highest value per unit of that scarce resource?
- Are there non-monetary benefits/risks I’m missing?
- Am I being swayed by sunk costs or defaults?

Communicating trade-offs
State choices in pairs: “We choose A; we forgo B (worth X).” This makes the invisible visible, reduces conflict, and sharpens decisions. It turns vague preferences into concrete comparisons and helps teams internalize that focus is a decision plus a cost.

Practice often. The more you quantify and name the runner-up, the clearer your choices become. Opportunity cost isn’t about guilt; it’s about clarity and alignment with what you value most.

Example

You can spend Saturday on: (A) a $600 freelance logo taking 8 hours, or (B) a portfolio overhaul taking 6 hours that is likely to raise your hourly rate by $10 for the next 100 hours of work this year.
- A nets $600/8 = $75 per hour now.
- B’s expected value: $10 x 100 = $1,000, over 6 hours ≈ $167 per hour.
Choosing A means giving up B’s best alternative value ($1,000). So the opportunity cost of A is $1,000; the opportunity cost of B is $600. If cash flow isn’t critical, B dominates at the margin.

Scenario

A small team can build either Feature X that delights 5,000 existing users and reduces churn by 1%, or Feature Y that could attract 300 new customers at $200 each but has 50% success probability. The team’s next two weeks (400 hours) is the scarce resource. They estimate X reduces churn by 1% of $1M ARR = $10,000 annual benefit with high certainty; Y’s expected value is 300 x $200 x 0.5 = $30,000. Per hour, X ≈ $25; Y ≈ $75. They choose Y, explicitly stating they forgo X’s $10,000 benefit as the opportunity cost. They schedule X next if Y underperforms, preserving option value.

Reflection Question

Where are you defaulting to a comfortable task whose true opportunity cost, if named and quantified, would immediately change your next hour?

MICROSOFT 365 ADMINISTRATOR (MS-102)
------------------------------------

Topic

Tenant architecture and service boundaries

Lesson

First principles
A Microsoft 365 tenant is a logical container for your organization’s identities, policies, and data in Microsoft’s cloud. Think layers: a global cloud fabric underneath, your tenant as a security and management boundary, and workloads (Exchange, SharePoint, OneDrive, Teams, etc.) running as services on top. Understanding where these layers begin and end helps you predict where data lives and how traffic flows.

Layered architecture mental model
- Global platform layer: Microsoft’s worldwide network, regions, front doors, and service infrastructure.
- Tenant layer: Your Microsoft Entra ID (identity), domains, licenses, policies; isolation from other tenants.
- Workload layer: Service instances for Exchange Online, SharePoint Online/OneDrive, Teams, etc., mapped to regions.
- Data layer: Data-at-rest locations per workload (mailboxes, site collections, files, chats, logs) with replication patterns and compliance controls.
- Client/edge layer: Users connect to the nearest Microsoft entry point; traffic then rides Microsoft’s backbone to the service region.

Regions, geos, and data residency
Microsoft organizes data centers into regions (e.g., West Europe) and geographies (Geo) like EU, US, UK, etc. Tenant creation ties you to a default Geo based on your billing address. By default, core data at rest (Exchange mailboxes, SharePoint/OneDrive content) resides in your home Geo. Multi-Geo can extend residency so subsets of users’ data live in additional regions to meet data residency needs.

Service boundaries
- Identity boundary: Microsoft Entra ID is tenant-scoped. Authentication, conditional access, and directory objects are isolated by tenant.
- Workload boundaries: Each service has its own data stores and compliance model. Exchange stores mailboxes; SharePoint/OneDrive store files and sites; Teams stores chat and channel messages in the Teams service with compliance copies integrated into the substrate and files in SharePoint/OneDrive.
- Admin boundaries: Some settings are tenant-wide (e.g., external sharing defaults), others are workload-specific (e.g., mailbox features, site policies).

Data location implications
- At rest: By default, core customer data is stored in your tenant’s home Geo unless configured with Multi-Geo. Exchange Online stores mailboxes in-region; SharePoint/OneDrive store site collections in-region; Teams files live in the associated SharePoint sites in-region; Teams chat is stored in the Teams service with data residency aligned to the Geo.
- In transit/processing: Data may be processed in other locations to deliver the service (e.g., transient processing, threat detection). Residency commitments focus on where data is stored at rest.

Routing and performance
User traffic typically enters Microsoft’s global network at the closest edge (front door), then travels on Microsoft’s backbone to the workload region. This reduces exposure to the public internet and often improves latency. Practical meaning: choose the right tenant Geo for the majority of users or enable Multi-Geo; front-door proximity helps, but long haul to a distant workload region still adds delay.

Compliance and sovereignty
If laws require data residency, validate which data types are covered (mail, files, Teams messages, audit logs) and whether Multi-Geo is needed. Understand cross-tenant collaboration: B2B and shared channels enable access across tenants without merging directories, but policies and audit obligations remain tenant-scoped. eDiscovery, retention, DLP, and sensitivity labels operate within and across workloads but enforce in tenant-defined boundaries.

Resilience and failover
Regions use high availability and regional pairing for disaster recovery. Know that failover may affect where processing occurs temporarily, while at-rest commitments remain anchored to the Geo. Design with service-level objectives, mailbox/database availability, site recycling, and versioning in mind rather than relying on manual backups.

Design checklist
- Where are most users located? Does default Geo fit, or do you need Multi-Geo?
- Which data types matter for residency? Mailboxes, files, Teams chat, audit logs.
- What latency-sensitive workloads do you have (voice/video in Teams, large file ops)?
- Do you need cross-tenant collaboration, or a single tenant with segmentation?
- Are governance, DLP, retention, and eDiscovery scoped correctly at tenant and workload layers?
- How will network egress enter Microsoft’s backbone quickly (local internet breakout preferred)?

Keep the layers clear: tenant for identity and policy, workloads for capabilities and storage, regions for residency and latency. That separation will guide correct choices and set expectations with stakeholders.

Example

A Canadian company acquires a German subsidiary. Legal requires EU residency for EU staff. They keep one global tenant for unified identity but enable Multi-Geo: EU users’ Exchange mailboxes and OneDrive/SharePoint sites are hosted in the EU Geo, while North American users remain in Canada/US regions. Teams is used company-wide; files for EU Teams live in EU-backed SharePoint sites. Guest access is allowed via B2B with German partners, governed by tenant-wide Conditional Access. Local internet breakout in both regions reduces latency to Microsoft’s front door.

Scenario

A startup with 70% users in the UK and 30% in Singapore is creating a new tenant. They choose the UK as the home Geo for residency and performance. They plan for growth in APAC and document a trigger (APAC reaches 45%) to evaluate Multi-Geo. For routing, they adopt local internet egress at both offices so traffic hits the nearest Microsoft edge. They set baseline policies in Microsoft Entra ID, configure Exchange and SharePoint with region-appropriate settings, and validate that Teams meeting performance is acceptable across both regions before finalizing rollout.

Reflection Question

In your environment, which matters more today—data residency assurance or latency—and how would that answer change your tenant, network, and workload configuration choices?

WISDOM
------

Topic

Attention and Noticing

Lesson

First principles
Attention is the mind’s steering wheel. It selects a tiny slice of reality for deep processing and lets everything else blur. What you notice shapes what you think is real, which shapes choices and results. Improving attention isn’t about trying harder; it’s about designing where the spotlight points and how stable it stays.

The spotlight and the sieve
Think of attention as a movable spotlight (what you select) and a sieve (what passes filters). Bottom-up pulls (novelty, notifications, threats) jerk the light around. Top-down aims (your goals) hold it steady. Skill is the ability to set the aim, widen or narrow the beam, and reduce involuntary pulls when it matters.

Common leaks
- Overstimulating environments: alerts, open tabs, cluttered rooms.
- Unnamed emotions: anxiety or excitement hijacks the beam.
- Vague goals: without a target, the spotlight wanders to the loudest thing.
- Fatigue: a dim light can’t hold steady.

Build noticing before control
You can’t steer what you don’t see drifting. Start with meta-attention: noticing where your attention is. Label it neutrally—“hearing,” “planning,” “worrying,” “itching,” “reading.” Labels create a gap between stimulus and reaction and make redirection easier.

Three drills
- 3x3 Scan (60 seconds): Look around and silently name 3 colors, 3 shapes, and 3 textures. Then shift to 3 sounds and 3 bodily sensations. This broadens the beam intentionally.
- Breath Box (2–3 minutes): Inhale 4, hold 4, exhale 4, hold 4. Count cycles. When you lose count, label “thinking,” return. This trains stability under gentle load.
- Name–Label–Choose (on demand): When you feel a pull, name it (“urge to check phone”), label the state (“restless”), and choose the next single step aligned with your goal. Choice reclaims agency.

Applying under pressure
Before a meeting or task, set a one-sentence aim: “Notice people’s questions more than my answers,” or “Find one surprising detail in this document.” During the task, use micro check-ins every 5–10 minutes: “Where is my beam? Is it on target?” If not, use one breath and one label to reset. Afterward, ask, “What did I miss?” to train your sieve.

Design the environment
- Default to silence: turn off non-essential notifications; put the phone in another room during focus blocks.
- Visual cues: a single object on your desk that represents today’s focus; remove unrelated items.
- Friction and glide: add friction to distractions (website blockers, logouts) and glide to targets (open the doc you’ll work on, pre-load references).

Attention diet
Just as food affects energy, inputs affect attention. Heavy novelty (doomscrolling) lowers your threshold for boredom. A cleaner feed—books, long-form, quiet walks—raises it. Protect the first and last 30 minutes of your day for low-noise inputs; this stabilizes the beam across the day.

A simple protocol
- One aim per block.
- 2–3 minutes of Breath Box.
- Remove one distraction.
- Set a 25-minute timer with a 1-minute mid-check.
- End with a 30-second debrief: name one thing you noticed that you usually miss. This trains the noticing muscle explicitly.

Attention is a practice. The win is not never drifting; it’s shortening the time between drift and noticing, and making the next good choice with less drama.

Example

On your commute, you usually ruminate and miss exits. Today you run a 3x3 Scan at each red light: 3 colors (the taxi’s yellow, the sky’s gray, the light’s green), 3 shapes (octagon stop sign, circular wheel, rectangular ad), 3 textures (worn leather wheel, smooth glass, rough denim). You notice your jaw is tight (label: tension). You loosen it, exhale, and feel more present. You still think about work, but you catch it within 10 seconds instead of 5 minutes and refocus on driving.

Scenario

You’re leading a one-hour status meeting that often derails. Before starting, you write the aim: “Notice uncertainty in voices more than the words.” You begin with Breath Box for one minute. During updates, you do mid-checks every 10 minutes. You catch a teammate’s slight pause before saying “on track.” You label internally: “hesitation.” You ask, “What risks are you watching?” A dependency surfaces early, averting a delay. After the meeting, you debrief: you noticed three nonverbal cues and missed one (someone checked out). You decide to scan the room left-to-right next time to widen your beam.

Reflection Question

What is one context this week where a tiny increase in noticing would change an outcome, and what single cue will you use to trigger that noticing?

FICTION-WRITING
---------------

Topic

Theme and Core Question

Lesson

First principles
Theme is the story’s contention about life. It’s not a topic (“love”) but an argument (“love without honesty destroys trust”). The cleanest way to hold theme is as a question the story will test: the core question. Readers come to stories for experiments with values; the plot is the lab.

Core question
Frame your theme as a contested question with stakes and tension. “Is freedom worth safety?” “What does ambition cost?” “Can people change?” The question should be answerable in more than one way so scenes can probe it from different angles.

Aligning premise with theme
Premise is what happens; theme is what it means. Coherence comes when the premise is tailor-made to test the question. If the theme is “What does loyalty owe the truth?”, a premise with competing loyalties (family vs whistleblowing at work) naturally generates choices that pressure your theme.

The Thematic Weave
Thread the question through key elements:
- Protagonist’s want vs need: Their want collides with the question; their need leads them toward an answer.
- Antagonistic force: Embodies the counter-answer. If your tentative answer is “freedom beats safety,” give the antagonist noble reasons for safety.
- Relationships: Allies and rivals express variations of the answer. Each subplot tests a facet.
- Setting and symbols: Environments can literalize the question (a walled city for safety vs freedom; a locked door motif that recurs whenever the character chooses).

Question–Answer Arc
Across acts, move from naive answer → pressure → reversal → earned answer.
- Act I: State the question in action. Show the easy, untested answer your protagonist believes.
- Act II: Escalate costs. Present good cases for the opposing answer. Force trade-offs.
- Act III: Make the protagonist pay the full price for a choice. The outcome (win/lose) is less important than the truth revealed by what they refuse to compromise.

Brainstorming scenes that express theme
Generate “experiments”: If the answer were X, what scene would prove it? If the answer were not X, what scene would disprove it? Aim for contrasts: a small, private test; a public, irreversible test; a moral temptation; a sacrifice scene. Tie each to plot turns so theme rides with momentum instead of pausing the story for speeches.

Keeping it from getting preachy
Let honest opposition speak well. Steelman the counterview. Show consequences rather than lectures. Use motifs and image systems to echo the question wordlessly (e.g., mirrors for identity, locked doors for freedom vs safety) so readers feel the argument.

A simple process
1) Draft your core question in one sentence.
2) Write a provisional answer (your hunch) and its strongest counter-argument.
3) List 5 scene ideas that would prove the counter-argument true.
4) Keep 2–3 of those in the outline so the story risks refuting your hunch.
5) Tag scenes with the facet of the question they test (e.g., loyalty vs truth at work/home/law).
6) In revision, cut or retune scenes that don’t move the question forward.

Theme is the spine. When every choice pressures the core question, the story feels inevitable and alive, even if readers disagree with the final answer.

Example

Core question: Is victory worth losing yourself?
Premise: A brilliant hacker enters a corporate bug-bounty competition where rivals play dirty.
- Protagonist wants to win to pay a sibling’s medical bills; believes ends justify means.
- Antagonist is a former mentor who wins clean, arguing the opposite.
Scene experiments:
- A tempting shortcut: install a backdoor on a competitor’s server. If taken, it proves “victory at any cost.”
- A mirror scene: the sibling notices the protagonist lying casually, asking, “Who are you becoming?”
- Public test: the protagonist can expose the mentor with a misleading edit. Doing so wins the round but stains their name.
- Final choice: sacrifice the win to reveal their own minor rule breach, preserving integrity. The earned answer: some victories aren’t worth the self you must kill to get them.

Scenario

You have a 60,000-word draft that feels scattered. You write the core question at the top of a whiteboard: “What does loyalty owe the truth?” You read each scene and stick a colored dot: blue if it tests loyalty, yellow if it tests truth, red if neither. A third of scenes are red. You cut or retune them so each either pressures a loyalty to a person (blue) or a loyalty to facts (yellow). You add a midpoint scene where the protagonist lies to protect a friend and a late scene where telling the truth would ruin that friend. The spine tightens; beta readers start talking about the theme unprompted.

Reflection Question

What is your story asking that you personally don’t have a settled answer to, and how can you design two honest scenes that argue powerfully for opposite answers?
