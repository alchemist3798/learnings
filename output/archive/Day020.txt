FILM-PHOTOGRAPHY
----------------

Topic

Planning a Shoot and Keeping a Photo Notebook

Lesson

First principles
Planning lowers uncertainty. Film gives you fewer chances and slower feedback, so you trade spontaneity for intention. A simple workflow pipeline and an external memory (your notebook) protect your ideas and exposures from being lost to haste.

Mental model: workflow pipeline
Think of your shoot as a series of gates: plan -> pack -> shoot -> log -> process -> contact -> select -> archive -> review. Each gate should be lightweight and repeatable. The outcome of one gate primes the next.

Mental model: external memory
Your head is for making choices, not for storing the last five meter readings. A pocket notebook or a notes template captures details you will forget later when contact sheets arrive.

Build a pre-shoot checklist
Design a one-page list you can reuse. Edit it as you learn.
1) Intent and constraints
- Story: subject, mood, why it matters now.
- Location and light: direction of light, reflectors in scene, time window.
- Style decisions: color vs B&W, grain tolerance, contrast preferences.
- Success criteria: what one frame would make the roll a win.

2) Exposure strategy
- Film stock and EI (exposure index): decide box speed or overexpose (e.g., HP5 at EI 320 for richer shadows).
- Metering plan: incident or spot; key tone placement; when to bracket.
- Filters: ND, yellow/orange/red, polarizer; compensation notes.
- Push/pull: note if lab should push +1 or pull -1 for the whole roll.

3) Gear and spares
- Bodies, lenses (with lens caps off when packing), meter, tripod, release.
- Batteries, gaffer tape, lens cloth, Sharpie, spare film.
- Light seals and shutter check for older cameras.

4) Logistics and safety
- Permits, model releases, property contacts.
- Weather plan B, cover from rain, hand warmers for winter.
- Travel time, parking, cash for meters, bathroom.
- Personal safety: work with a buddy if remote; emergency contact.

5) Contingency
- If sun: use backlight + reflector.
- If overcast: lean into soft portraits, open shade textures.
- If gear fails: downgrade plan using one body/one lens.

Keep a shot log
Your notebook captures both what you did and why you did it. A simple spread works:
- Roll header: date, location, camera, lens, film, EI, push/pull, lab.
- For each frame: frame number, meter read, chosen exposure (shutter/aperture), filter, focus distance, scene description, intent, any bracket, and a quick quality note.
- Codes speed writing: L=low light, BKT=bracket, POL=polarizer, OY=orange filter, NF=no filter, S=subject, BG=background, INT=intent.

Why it matters: When you hold a contact sheet weeks later, you will not remember why frame 19 is one stop brighter. Your log links the creative bet (intent) to the technical choice (exposure). That loop builds taste and control.

How to log fast
- Pre-draw a grid for 36 rows.
- Write exposure as '500 f4' instead of complete notation.
- Use arrows for brackets: 1/3 or 2/3 stop up/down.
- Describe intent in five words: 'silhouette mother + child'.

Organize negatives and contact sheets
Aim for findability in 30 seconds.
1) After development
- Cut 35mm into 5- or 6-frame strips; sleeve in archival pages.
- Label a unique roll ID: YYMMDD-Location-Roll#. Write on the sleeve edge, not the film.
- Add a page in a binder sorted by year/month.

2) Contact sheets and proofs
- Make a contact sheet or low-res scan for every roll.
- Put roll ID on the sheet. Circle selects in grease pencil or annotate in your notes app.
- Star system: * maybe, ** strong, *** must print.

3) Cross-reference
- In your notebook, index: roll ID -> page in binder -> contact sheet file name.
- In your digital folder, mirror the roll ID in filenames: 2406-Paris-02_frame19.jpg.

4) Preservation
- Store binders upright in a cool, dry place.
- Keep a second copy of scans on a separate drive or cloud.

Feedback loop
Schedule a 15-minute review per roll when contacts arrive:
- Compare log intent with actual frames. Did exposure deliver the mood?
- Note film stock character in this light. Adjust EI next time if shadows are thin or highlights too dense.
- Write one improvement for the next shoot: 'Bracket skin in backlight +2/3' or 'Ask subjects to pause mid-laugh.'

Keep it simple
Your system should survive rain, fatigue, and excitement. One good checklist, one repeatable log, and consistent labeling will raise your keeper rate more than any new lens.

Example

Shot log snippet (Roll ID 2407-Seaside-01)
- Frame 12: 1/500 f4, NF, 3m. S: surfer paddling. INT: isolate triangle of board. Meter: incident +1/3 over. Note: speculars bright, try polarizer next.
- Frame 13 (BKT +1/3): 1/320 f4, NF. INT: hold highlights on foam. Note: better skin tone.
- Frame 19: 1/125 f8, OY filter, 5m. S: pier silhouette at dusk. INT: high contrast graphic. Note: meter sky -1; print on grade 3.
- Frame 27: 1/60 f2, NF, 1m. S: couple under awning. INT: tender close-up, rain bokeh. Note: slight motion blur, bring tripod next time.

Scenario

You plan a late afternoon portrait session in a park with fast-changing clouds. The night before, you run the checklist: decide on Portra 400 at EI 200 for softer tones; bring 85mm and 50mm, a silver reflector, and a step stool. You write contingencies: if sun, backlight hair and fill with reflector; if overcast, shoot near pale walls to lift exposure. You pre-draw a 36-frame grid in your notebook and set codes for quick logging. On location, you meter faces for midtone, then open up 2/3 stop to protect skin. As clouds roll in, you switch to the overcast plan and note 'diffuse light, reflect off path.' During the session you log only essentials: frame, exposure, distance, one-line intent. After processing, you file sleeves under 2407-CentralPark-01. The contact sheet shows three near-keepers with gentle skin; your notes explain why frame 18 glows (cloud edge, reflector angle). You star it ***, mark 'print at 10x8,' and write a review line: 'Next time, start with stool height for eyes to camera; it fixed posture fast.'

Reflection Question

Which single line in your pre-shoot checklist would most reduce your risk of coming home with technically fine but emotionally empty frames, and how will you make it impossible to skip that line on a rushed day?

MICROECONOMICS
--------------

Topic

Short-Run vs Long-Run in Markets

Lesson

First principles
Time changes what is possible. In the short run, some inputs are fixed, so firms squeeze more out of what they already have. In the long run, all inputs can adjust, and new firms can enter or exit. Prices and profits behave differently across these horizons because flexibility changes.

Short run: capacity is sticky
- At least one input is fixed (often plant size, key equipment, permits, trained staff). Firms can vary labor hours, intensity, and materials, but not total capacity.
- Decision rule for a price-taking firm: produce the quantity where price roughly equals marginal cost if price covers variable cost. If price falls below average variable cost, shut down temporarily, because operating would lose more than staying idle.
- Profits can be positive, zero, or negative. Fixed costs are sunk in the short run; a firm might operate at a loss if price covers variable costs and contributes something toward fixed costs.
- Supply is relatively inelastic. When demand jumps, quantity cannot expand much immediately; prices rise sharply.

Long run: capacity can change and firms can come and go
- All inputs are variable. Firms can build new plants, adopt new technology, renegotiate contracts, train people, switch suppliers.
- Entry and exit: positive economic profits attract entrants; losses push firms to exit. With free entry/exit in a competitive industry, long-run equilibrium tends toward zero economic profit (normal return). Price gravitates to the minimum of average total cost for the marginal firm.
- Industry supply is more elastic. Over time, quantity expands more and prices settle.

Industry differences
- Constant-cost industry: input prices don't change much as industry grows (e.g., digital goods with scalable cloud). Entry shifts supply out; long-run price returns near its original level.
- Increasing-cost industry: inputs get pricier as the industry expands (e.g., limited skilled labor, scarce land). Long-run price ends higher than before.
- Decreasing-cost industry: learning and scale reduce input costs (e.g., solar panels over decades). Long-run price ends lower than before.

Dynamic adjustment story
1) Demand shock up: In the short run, price spikes; firms run extra shifts, cut maintenance windows, use overtime. Profits rise.
2) Signal and response: Seeing profits, existing firms order equipment; new firms plan entry. There are lags: permitting, construction, hiring, learning curves.
3) Long run: Supply increases as capacity goes live. Competition erodes economic profit; price falls toward long-run cost. Quantity sold is higher than before.
4) If the demand increase was temporary, some added capacity becomes excess; exit or repurposing follows.

Why entry/exit matter to you
- Persistent high profits are rare in competitive markets. If you see them, ask what barrier to entry maintains them (patents, network effects, regulation, brand, switching costs). No barrier usually means profits invite entry and fade.
- Capacity expansion timing drives strategy. Ordering capital too late misses high-margin months; ordering too early risks idle assets.

Practical heuristics
- Look for lead times: how long to add one unit of capacity? Weeks for a coffee cart, years for a chip fab.
- Ask: are input markets flexible? If a few specialized suppliers exist, short-run bottlenecks last longer.
- Monitor exit barriers: long leases, specialized equipment, or pride slow exit, keeping prices lower for longer after a boom ends.

Costs and shutdown in plain terms
- Fixed costs: costs you pay even if you make zero units this month (rent, basic insurance). Short run: ignore when deciding to produce today.
- Variable costs: costs that rise with output (ingredients, hourly labor). If price covers these, making some output can be smart even if you're not covering all fixed costs yet.

Putting it together
- Short run: price does most of the adjusting; quantity adjusts a little. Profits can appear or vanish quickly.
- Long run: quantity does most of the adjusting through capacity and entry/exit; price drifts toward underlying costs.

Common mistakes
- Confusing zero economic profit with 'no profit.' In economics, normal profit is included in cost; zero economic profit can still pay owners a fair return.
- Assuming today's price spike lasts. Without barriers, expect new supply to arrive and pressure prices down.
- Ignoring adjustment costs. Even 'flexible' industries face hiring, training, and coordination frictions that stretch the short run.

Example

A new stadium opens in a small town. On game days, demand for pizza surges. Short run: existing pizzerias run ovens nonstop, pay overtime, and raise prices from $14 to $18 because capacity is fixed. Lines form; profits jump. Over the next year, two food trucks and one new pizzeria enter. Long run: total slices sold per game day doubles, but competition pulls the average price back to $15–$16, close to the cost of making a pie plus a normal return. The original pizzerias keep some loyal fans but no longer earn outsized profits each game.

Scenario

A startup makes hand sanitizer when a sudden health scare hits. In month one, demand explodes. Short run: bottles, caps, and ethanol are bottlenecks; the firm runs 24/7 shifts and charges a high price. Profits look amazing. Competitors notice and line up suppliers. Three months later, new filling lines arrive across the industry. Long run: shelves fill, prices fall near cost, and some latecomers exit with leftover inventory. The startup that signed a one-year warehouse lease feels exit barriers and must discount heavily to clear stock.

Reflection Question

When you see a price spike in your industry, what concrete evidence would convince you that it reflects a short-run bottleneck rather than a lasting shift in long-run costs?

MICROSOFT 365 ADMINISTRATOR (MS-102)
------------------------------------

Topic

Conditional Access fundamentals

Lesson

First principles
Zero Trust says: never trust, always verify, enforce least privilege. Conditional Access (CA) is the policy engine in Microsoft Entra ID that evaluates signals about a sign-in and applies controls. Instead of a blunt allow/deny, CA asks: who is this, what are they trying to access, from where and on what, and under what risk? Then it shapes access: block, require MFA, require a compliant device, limit sessions, and more.

Building blocks
1) Assignments (who and what)
- Users and groups: include or exclude specific users, groups, or roles. Always keep at least one break-glass account excluded from all CA policies.
- Workload identities: service principals and managed identities can also be targeted with separate considerations.
- Cloud apps or user actions: target 'All cloud apps' or selected apps like Exchange Online, SharePoint Online, Salesforce, etc. You can also target user actions such as 'Register security information' to protect MFA/SSPR enrollment flows.

2) Conditions (when and how)
- Sign-in risk and user risk: risk signals (with the right licenses) based on unusual activity.
- Device platform: Windows, macOS, iOS, Android, Linux.
- Locations: named locations like trusted corporate egress IP ranges; you can mark locations as trusted.
- Client apps: browser, modern auth clients, legacy protocols. Blocking legacy authentication is foundational.
- Device filters: include/exclude by device properties (for example, device ownership, join type).

3) Controls (what to enforce)
- Grant controls: Block; or Allow only if one or more requirements are met. Common requirements: require multi-factor authentication; require device to be marked as compliant (Intune); require Hybrid Azure AD joined device; require password change (when risk justifies it); require terms of use; require approved client app; require app protection policy; require specific authentication strength.
- Session controls: Sign-in frequency (re-auth cadence), Persistent browser session, Application enforced restrictions (limit download in SharePoint/OneDrive), and Conditional Access App Control (proxy via Defender for Cloud Apps).

Policy evaluation mental model
- A sign-in is evaluated against all enabled policies. If a user/app pair matches the assignments and the conditions evaluate to true, the policy applies. Multiple applicable policies combine: blocks win; required grant controls accumulate (the user must satisfy the union: MFA and compliant device, for example). Report-only mode lets you simulate impact without enforcement.

Common baseline intentions
Legacy 'baseline policies' in preview were retired; many organizations now either enable Security defaults or define a small, opinionated CA set. Typical starting goals:
- Block legacy authentication globally. Outcome: stops basic auth and legacy protocols that cannot do modern MFA.
- Protect privileged roles. Require MFA for directory, workload, and Azure admins, from any location and any client.
- Protect all users off trusted networks or when accessing high-value apps. Require MFA if location is not trusted or if sign-in risk is medium/high.
- Protect data on devices. For SharePoint/OneDrive/Teams, require compliant device or an approved client with app protection; optionally use session controls to block download on unmanaged devices.
- Exclusions: break-glass accounts, emergency access process, and service accounts that cannot do MFA (ideally moved to workload identities instead).

Rollout approach
- Design: write policies in a standard template (Name, Purpose, Scope, Conditions, Controls, Exclusions, Owner, Version). Keep scopes small at first.
- Simulate: set policies to Report-only and monitor the sign-in logs and the What If tool.
- Stage: pilot with IT, then a security champions group, then expand. Communicate the 'why' and give MFA enrollment help.
- Guardrails: at least two emergency access accounts, excluded from CA, with long strong passwords stored securely and used only in break-glass events.
- Iterate: tighten locations, tune sign-in frequency, and shift from 'MFA for admins' to 'MFA for everyone, risk/location aware' as users enroll.

Pitfalls and fixes
- Lockout risk: enabling 'All users' + 'All cloud apps' + Block without exclusions. Fix: report-only, phased scopes, and maintained break-glass accounts.
- Ignoring legacy auth: attackers love basic auth. Fix: explicitly block legacy protocols and disable basic auth endpoints.
- Over-relying on trusted IPs: VPNs and cloud work blur perimeters. Fix: use risk-based signals and device compliance, not only network location.
- Service accounts: human accounts with MFA disabled are risky. Fix: convert to workload identities with appropriate policies.

What success looks like
- Most users complete MFA enrollment.
- Legacy auth attempts drop to near zero.
- High-value apps allow access only from compliant or protected clients.
- Incident response can contain compromised credentials by stepping up auth or blocking at the policy edge.

Example

Starter CA set for a 200-user firm
1) Block Legacy Auth: Users=All, Exclude=BreakGlass-01/02, Apps=All, Conditions=Client apps (legacy), Control=Block.
2) Admin MFA Always: Users=Directory roles (Privileged roles), Apps=All, Control=Require MFA; Session=Sign-in frequency 8 hours.
3) MFA Off-Trusted-Network: Users=All, Exclude=BreakGlass, Apps=All, Conditions=Locations not in 'HQ-Egress', Control=Require MFA.
4) Protect SharePoint/OneDrive: Users=All, Apps=Office 365 SharePoint Online, Control=Require compliant device OR approved client with app protection; Session=Use app enforced restrictions to block download on unmanaged devices.

Scenario

A contractor needs short-term access to SharePoint. You create a group 'Ext-Contractors' and a targeted policy: Assignments=Users in Ext-Contractors; Apps=SharePoint Online; Conditions=Locations Any; Grant=Require approved client app and app protection policy; Session=Use app enforced restrictions to block download. You exclude BreakGlass accounts. You run the policy in report-only for two days, confirm no impact on employees, then enable it. Contractors can view and edit in the mobile or desktop app, but browser downloads on unmanaged devices are blocked.

Reflection Question

If you had to remove one control from your baseline CA set to reduce user friction, which would you choose and what compensating signal or scope change would you add so risk does not meaningfully increase?

WISDOM
------

Topic

Aligning Actions with Values

Lesson

First principles
Values are directions, not destinations. They tell you which way to face; actions move your feet. The gap between what we say we value and what we do exists because intentions are abstract while life is concrete. Implementation intentions bridge the gap by binding a cue in the world to a specific behavior in a context. Accountability turns a private wish into a public commitment with feedback.

Clarify values into behaviors
- Pick your top three values for this season (for example: health, curiosity, kindness). Write a sentence for each in plain language: 'Health means I maintain energy to play with my kids.'
- Translate each into two or three concrete behaviors. Make them observable: 'Walk 30 minutes after lunch' beats 'be more active.'
- Define bright lines: small rules that remove debate. 'No phone at the table' is clearer than 'be present at dinner.'
- Name anti-behaviors that suck you off-course (doomscrolling in bed, saying yes without calendar check).

Implementation intentions (If–Then)
This is a simple script: If [cue], then I will [behavior] in [place] for [duration].
- Health: If I put my coffee mug down after lunch, then I will put on shoes and walk the block for 10 minutes.
- Curiosity: If I open my laptop at 8 pm, then I will read one paper note before any video.
- Kindness: If I feel the urge to correct someone at work, then I will ask one clarifying question first.
Tie the behavior to an existing routine (habit stacking) and choose a tiny version you can always do. Tiny keeps streaks alive when days go sideways.

Friction design
- Make the good easy: lay out walking shoes, pin the paper you want to read, pre-draft a kind opener line.
- Make the misaligned hard: remove social apps from your phone, set a DNS block for distracting sites during key hours, store the TV remote in another room.
- Use environment labels: a sticky note that says 'Walk after lunch' on your mug is cheap and effective.

Accountability mechanisms
- Scoreboard: a visible tracker with only yes/no marks for each behavior. Keep it where you will see it (fridge, desk, watch face).
- Pact: tell a person you respect what you will do and when. Send a one-line check-in after you do it.
- Consequence: pre-commit a small cost for missing two days in a row (donate to a cause you dislike, or do a chore for your partner). Keep stakes low but real.
- Social proof: join a small group with the same value-aligned behaviors; borrow their momentum when yours dips.

Obstacle planning
Assume friction. Write backup If–Then scripts for your common derailers.
- If it rains at lunch, then I will walk inside the stairwell for 10 minutes.
- If I work late, then I will read one page of a saved article instead of a full paper.
- If a meeting turns heated, then I will pause for one breath before speaking.

Review loop
Once a week, do a 10-minute value alignment review.
- Look at your scoreboard. Which behaviors were easy because of design? Which failed because cues were vague or friction was high?
- Adjust scripts: sharpen the cue, shrink the behavior, or move the time/place.
- Celebrate consistency over intensity. Small, certain actions compound into identity: 'I am a person who walks after lunch.'

Avoid common traps
- Vague values: 'Be better' creates no action. Write clear sentences and behaviors.
- Moral licensing: doing one value-aligned act does not buy permission to drift all day. Keep bright lines.
- Overload: pick one behavior per value to start. Add more only after it feels automatic.

Putting it together
1) Choose three values and write a sentence for each.
2) Pick one concrete behavior per value.
3) Write an If–Then for each, attach it to a current routine, and place a physical cue in the environment.
4) Create a tiny scoreboard and one accountability pact.
5) Review weekly and iterate. Alignment is a practice, not a single decision.

Example

Value: Curiosity
- Behavior: Read something outside my field nightly.
- If–Then: If it is 9:15 pm and I sit on the couch, then I will read one page of a saved article in Pocket for 5 minutes before any video.
- Friction design: TV remote lives in a different room; the tablet opens to Pocket by default.
- Accountability: Text a friend a one-line takeaway every Friday night. If I miss two Fridays in a row, I buy them a book of their choice.

Scenario

A project manager values integrity and family time during a high-pressure product launch. They create two scripts: If a stakeholder asks for an off-the-record change, then I will reply, 'Let's document the impact and bring it to the steering group.' If I arrive home after 7 pm, then I will put my phone in the kitchen drawer and sit at the table for 20 minutes with my kids. They place a sticky note on their laptop lid with the first line for tough conversations and put a phone tray by the kitchen door. Every Thursday they send a two-sentence update to their mentor about one hard choice handled with integrity and one dinner fully present. The mentor replies with a thumbs-up or a probing question. Over three weeks, choices feel easier because the cues, words, and consequences are pre-decided.

Reflection Question

Which one small If–Then script, placed at the right moment and location, would change the tone of your day most reliably, and what friction could you add or remove to make it automatic?

FICTION-WRITING
---------------

Topic

Project: Character-Driven Vignette

Lesson

First principles
A vignette is a focused slice of life that reveals a character through a choice under pressure. It is not about plot complexity; it is about a vivid moment, a small arc (a tilt in belief or resolve), a coherent point of view, and dialogue that carries subtext. In a short space, you integrate goal, stakes, and obstacle so the reader feels a shift.

Define the core
- Character: who they are in one sentence (role + contradiction). Example: a meticulous line cook who lies to avoid conflict.
- Goal: what they want right now, not in life. Make it concrete (return a ring, avoid a phone call, hide a stain).
- Stakes: what happens if they fail (lose face, confirm fear, harm someone else).
- Obstacle: external (someone or something in the way) and internal (a belief or wound that binds their hands).

Choose point of view and distance
- Decide POV and tense before drafting. First person present is intimate and urgent; close third past is flexible and polished. Stick to one camera in the character's head; filter every detail through it. Voice flows from vocabulary, rhythm, and what this person notices.

Design the pressure cooker
- Setting that turns the screw: a quiet bus at midnight, a crowded kitchen during dinner rush, a hospital hallway at shift change. Choose a setting that forces the character to act now.

Beat map for a 800–1200 word vignette
1) Hook image in motion: start where the pressure touches the character (sizzle of oil, a ringtone in a church).
2) Spark: a small event that demands a response (boss asks a question, ex texts, a child tugs a sleeve).
3) Escalation: two or three beats that tighten options. Use actions and dialogue to reveal values and fear.
4) Decision: the character chooses, even if by inaction.
5) Consequence: a small but clear change in the world or in the character's stance.
6) Afterglow image: a resonant detail that carries the new tilt.

Dialogue and subtext
- Use speech to collide agendas. Give each speaker a mini-goal per exchange. Let what is not said hum under what is said. Use action beats to stage emotion: hands, breath, the way someone folds a napkin.
- Tag lightly. Prefer action beats over endless 'he said/she said.' Keep voices distinct: word choice, sentence length, metaphors they would use.

Internal vs external conflict
- External: the burned steak, the closed office door, the missed train.
- Internal: the rule they live by (never disappoint) vs the need now (tell the truth). Show it through perception and micro-choices, not lectures.

Drafting sequence (Macro-to-Micro)
- Pass 1: Beat outline. One sentence per beat. Confirm goal, stakes, obstacle.
- Pass 2: Fast draft in your chosen POV/tense. Do not correct lines yet. Get to the decision and consequence.
- Pass 3 (macro): Read aloud. Mark where momentum sags. Sharpen beats; raise stakes by making costs specific.
- Pass 4 (voice): Make diction consistent with the character. Replace generic verbs with precise ones they would think. Cut throat-clearing and explainers.
- Pass 5 (polish): Trim 10%. Remove adverbs propping weak verbs. Ensure every line either advances action, reveals character, or paints setting.

Consistency checklist
- POV never leaks other minds.
- Tense stays steady.
- Goal present in early lines; decision visible late.
- Dialogue reveals more than it states; action beats carry emotion.
- Final image echoes the new tilt.

Practical timebox
- 10 minutes: outline beats.
- 30 minutes: draft.
- 20 minutes: macro revision.
- 20 minutes: voice and polish.

What to aim for
A reader should finish and think: I know who this person is, I felt their squeeze, and something in them shifted—even slightly.

Example

Mini outline + micro-sample
- Character: Noor, a grocery cashier who never corrects customers.
- Goal: prevent her younger brother from seeing their father drunk on a livestream.
- Stakes: if she fails, her brother's trust in adults cracks more.
- Obstacle: a line of customers; her fear of making scenes.
- Setting: checkout lane at 5:45 pm, raincoats dripping, manager prowling.
- Beats: Hook (scanner beeps, phone buzzes); Spark (text: 'Dad is live again'); Escalation (manager posts at her lane, a customer argues about a coupon, the video volume auto-plays); Decision (she kills her light and steps off script); Consequence (she calls her brother from the stockroom and tells the truth about their dad); Afterglow (returns to her lane, scans in silence, hears only her own steady breath).
Micro-sample:
Customer: 'It says buy-one-get-one.'
Noor smiles too wide. 'I can check the tag.' Her phone hums. Dad's laugh, tinny, high. Kam's name at the top. Noor's finger hovers. Rain spots run down the plexi.
Manager leans in. 'Keep the line moving.'
Noor reaches for the light switch over her lane. The beeps stop. 'I need five minutes,' she says, voice small but holding. She walks toward the stockroom, dialing.

Scenario

You have 50 minutes on a commuter train. You open Notes and write a one-sentence character with a contradiction, then a 6-beat outline. You pick first person present to keep the camera tight. You draft fast, aiming for the decision by minute 30. On a quick macro pass, you cut a backstory paragraph and add a small cost to the decision: the manager will dock an hour. On the voice pass, you replace 'walks quickly' with 'shoulders the swinging door' and change a generic 'nervous' to a hand that cannot stop folding the paper receipt. You end on an image: the receipt now flat on the counter like a flag.

Reflection Question

What single beat in your vignette most clearly forces your character to reveal who they are, and how can you tighten the setting or dialogue so that no safe, vague choice is possible in that moment?
