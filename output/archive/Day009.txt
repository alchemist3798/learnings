FILM-PHOTOGRAPHY
----------------

Topic

Project: Shoot Your First Roll

Lesson

Purpose
Today you’ll shoot a complete roll with intention, notes, and a simple plan-do-review loop. The goal is not perfect photos; it’s to build a repeatable field workflow that turns mistakes into learning.

Choose Film and Camera Settings
Pick a forgiving film. Color negative 400 ISO (e.g., Portra 400 or Kodak Gold 200/400) or black-and-white 400 (e.g., Tri-X, HP5+) is ideal. Color negative likes a bit of extra light, so consider rating ISO 400 at 200 or 320 (set the camera’s ISO accordingly) to give it 1/3 to 1 stop more exposure. If you have slide film, do the opposite (meter carefully and err slightly toward underexposure). Ensure batteries are fresh, shutter speeds sound plausible, and the light seals are intact.

Before You Leave
- Load the film carefully; advance to frame 1 and check the rewind knob turns when you wind, proving the film is engaged.
- Set ISO to your chosen rating.
- Clean the lens; set a baseline aperture (e.g., f/8) and shutter speed (e.g., 1/250) for daylight.
- Prepare a tiny log: paper card or phone note with columns: frame #, ISO, f/, shutter, metering (spot/center/Sunny 16), scene description, composition intent, +/− exposure compensation.

Plan the Roll (Plan-Do-Review)
Plan: Pre-allocate your 24–36 frames into learning buckets so you see patterns in the results.
- Light variety (12): open sun, light shade, deep shade, backlight, window light, indoor ambient.
- Subject variety (8): portrait, architecture, texture, motion, close-up, landscape, street.
- Composition intent (6): leading lines, frame within frame, rule of thirds, symmetry, negative space.
- Experiment (rest): bracketing, intentional blur, silhouette.
This prevents “random walk” shooting and gives you comparable sets A/B/C to review later.

Metering in the Field
First principles: exposure = how much light hits the film. Shutter controls motion; aperture controls depth of field; ISO is film sensitivity (fixed for the roll). For negative film, meter for the shadows; for slide/digital, protect highlights. If you have a meter, point it at the light falling on your subject (incident) or at a mid-tone area (reflected). Without a meter, Sunny 16 works: in full sun at ISO 400, use 1/400 at f/16 (or equivalent like 1/1000 at f/8). In open shade, open 2 stops from sun; in heavy shade, 3–4 stops. Backlight often needs +1 to +2 stops for negatives.

Composition on Purpose
Pick one clear subject per frame. Ask: where do I want eyes to land, and what clutter can I exclude? Use edges deliberately: set your frame, then scan the borders to remove distractions. Align strong lines; use thirds for balance or break it with intention (center a symmetrical subject). If it’s a portrait, watch the background merge; move your feet more than your zoom.

During the Shoot
- Slow down: expose fewer frames but with reasons. Note your intention in the log before or right after the shot.
- Bracket smartly: for tricky light, take one at meter, one +1 stop, one −1 stop (especially if unsure).
- Control motion: if subjects move, keep shutter ≥ 1/250; if you want blur, drop to 1/15–1/30 and brace.
- Focus deliberately: on eyes for portraits; hyperfocal for street/landscape (stop to f/8–f/11).
- Don’t open the camera back until you’ve rewound fully. Rewind when the tension releases and the counter is near 0.

After the Shoot (Review Setup)
- Lab instructions: standard development, no push/pull unless you rated the whole roll differently. If you shot ISO 400 at 320, do not push; that’s intentional overexposure for negative film.
- Get scans/contact sheet. Save your log next to the images.
- First pass review: look at exposure density. Are shadows rich? Highlights blown? Motion blur where unintended?
- Second pass review: match frames to log. Did metering choices match outcomes? Did compositions serve the subject?

Feedback Loops
- If most frames are thin (underexposed), add +2/3 stop next roll or rate film slower. If highlights collapsed on slide film, reduce exposure 1/3–2/3 stop.
- If motion blur ruined photos, raise your minimum shutter speed rule.
- If compositions feel busy, simplify backgrounds and move closer.
Small, explicit adjustments are the engine of progress.

Mindset
Treat this roll as a lab experiment. Each frame pays a tuition in information. Curiosity beats perfection, and notes turn curiosity into skill.

Example

A single frame log entry:
Frame 12 — ISO 320 (Portra 400 rated at 320). Aperture f/4, Shutter 1/250. Metering: center-weighted, +1 stop for backlight. Scene: Portrait of friend under a tree with bright sky behind. Composition intent: subject’s face framed by branches; background blown soft. Reasoning: Negative film likes more light; backlight risks underexposed face, so I added +1. Focus on near eye. Anticipated result: pleasing skin tones, soft background, sky may go bright, acceptable.

Scenario

You plan a Saturday afternoon walk with a 36-exposure roll. You divide it: 10 frames in open sun on the main street, 10 frames in open shade in the park, 8 frames indoors at a cafe, 4 frames for experiments, and 4 for portraits. You load Portra 400, set ISO to 320, and carry a small paper log. In the cafe, light is dim, so you choose 1/60 at f/2.8 and brace your elbows on the table, logging the choice. In the park, you practice backlit leaves: meter the grass (mid-tone), add +1 stop, and bracket one shot. Later, your scans show cafe shots slightly blurry; next time you set a personal rule: never below 1/125 handheld unless braced or with image stabilization.

Reflection Question

When metering a high-contrast scene with negative film, how would you bias exposure to protect what matters most in the final image, and what trade-off are you consciously accepting?

MICROECONOMICS
--------------

Topic

Supply and Demand Basics

Lesson

First Principles
Markets coordinate buyers and sellers through prices. Demand describes how much buyers want at each price; supply describes how much sellers will produce at each price. The intersection is equilibrium: a price where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied. At that price, no one has an unfulfilled gain from trade left on the table.

Why Demand Slopes Down
As price falls, more people find the good worth it, and existing buyers may buy more. Two forces drive this: substitution (cheaper relative to alternatives) and diminishing marginal willingness to pay (each additional unit is worth a bit less to you). Plot price on the vertical axis and quantity on the horizontal; the demand curve slopes downward.

Why Supply Slopes Up
As price rises, more producers cover their costs, and existing firms expand output. Higher output often means using costlier inputs or less efficient capacity, so marginal cost tends to rise with quantity. The supply curve slopes upward.

Equilibrium and Tendency
Where supply meets demand, the market clears. If price is above equilibrium, a surplus appears; sellers cut prices to move inventory. If price is below equilibrium, a shortage appears; buyers bid up the price. This push and pull tends to move price toward equilibrium, even if it overshoots in the short run.

Movements vs. Shifts
A change in the good’s own price moves you along a curve. A change in anything else shifts the curve.
- Demand shifters: income (normal vs inferior goods), tastes, prices of related goods (substitutes and complements), expectations of future prices/income, number of buyers, and information.
- Supply shifters: input costs (wages, materials), technology/productivity, expectations, number of sellers, taxes/subsidies, regulations, and shocks (weather, strikes).

Predicting Effects of Shocks
Use comparative statics: hold other things equal, shift one curve, and infer direction of new equilibrium.
- Demand increases (right shift): price up, quantity up.
- Demand decreases (left shift): price down, quantity down.
- Supply increases (right shift): price down, quantity up.
- Supply decreases (left shift): price up, quantity down.
When both shift, one outcome may be ambiguous. For example, if demand rises and supply falls, price definitely rises, but quantity could rise or fall depending on which shift is larger.

Elasticity Matters (Light Touch)
Elasticity is curve steepness. Steeper demand means buyers are less responsive to price (inelastic). With inelastic demand, a leftward supply shock (like a bad harvest) raises price a lot while quantity falls modestly. You don’t need numbers to grasp direction; just picture steeper or flatter lines.

Drawing Tips
- Label axes clearly: P on vertical, Q on horizontal.
- Draw original S and D, mark intersection E0.
- Shift one curve, draw new intersection E1.
- Use arrows to indicate up/down in P and Q.
This visual helps you reason even without precise data.

Short Run vs. Long Run
In the short run, capacity is sticky; supply is more inelastic. Over time, firms enter/exit and adjust technology, making supply more elastic. Demand elasticity can also change with habits and available substitutes. The same shock can have big short-run price spikes that dampen over time.

Edge Cases and Caveats
- Price controls (ceilings/floors) prevent the price from moving to equilibrium, causing persistent shortages or surpluses.
- Expectations can loop back: if buyers expect future price hikes, current demand may jump today.
- Quality changes and hidden features can shift perceived value (a taste shift) even if the physical good is similar.

Mental Model
Equilibrium is a balance point; comparative statics is the exercise of nudging one side and observing how the balance tilts. Keep track of what moved and what stayed put. Simple, consistent diagrams are your best ally.

Example

Coffee frost shock: A frost damages coffee trees in Brazil. This raises input scarcity and production difficulty, shifting supply left. Demand for coffee stays roughly the same in the short run. Prediction: equilibrium price rises; equilibrium quantity falls. If demand is inelastic (coffee drinkers cling to their habit), price may spike significantly while cups sold drop less. Over time, farmers replant, other countries expand output (supply becomes more elastic), and prices ease toward a new normal.

Scenario

A local bakery sells 200 bagels a day at $2. A popular food blogger posts a rave review. The next morning, a line forms around the block. This is a demand shift right: the willingness to buy at each price rose. If the bakery keeps the price at $2, it sells out early (shortage). If it raises price to $2.50, it rations the limited morning capacity, moving toward a new equilibrium with higher price and higher total sold up to its oven limits. A month later, the bakery adds a second oven (supply shift right). With more capacity, the equilibrium price falls somewhat and quantity rises further, easing the lines.

Reflection Question

When you observe both a surge in sales and a rising price in a market, what evidence would you seek to decide whether you’re seeing a demand increase, a supply decrease, or both?

MICROSOFT 365 ADMINISTRATOR (MS-102)
------------------------------------

Topic

Role based administration concepts

Lesson

First Principles: RBAC and Least Privilege
Role-Based Access Control (RBAC) grants permissions through roles that match tasks, not people. Least privilege means giving the minimum access needed, only for as long as needed, and no broader than needed. Together, they reduce blast radius: mistakes or compromises stay contained.

Scope and Time
Good RBAC answers three questions: who, what, and where/when. Who is the principal (user, group, service principal). What is the role (the allowed actions). Where is the scope (tenant-wide, app-specific, or limited via administrative units). When is the time window (permanent vs. just-in-time with approvals). Least privilege sharpens each dimension: narrow role, narrow scope, limited time.

Common Microsoft 365 Roles to Know
- Global Administrator: full control of Microsoft Entra ID (Azure AD) and many M365 services. Keep this rare.
- Global Reader: tenant-wide read-only; great for audits and training.
- User Administrator: manage users and groups, reset passwords, not full tenant control.
- Helpdesk Administrator (Password Administrator): reset non-admin passwords, manage support tickets.
- Exchange Administrator: manage mailboxes and Exchange Online settings.
- SharePoint Administrator: manage sites, sharing, and OneDrive.
- Teams Administrator: manage Teams policies, org-wide settings.
- Security Administrator and Security Reader: manage or view security features and alerts.
- Compliance Administrator and Compliance Data Administrator: manage policies, retention, DLP, eDiscovery.
- Billing Administrator: manage subscriptions and licenses.
- Intune (Endpoint) Administrator: device, app, and compliance management.
- Conditional Access Administrator and Authentication Administrator: manage sign-in policies and unlocks.
- Privileged Role Administrator (or Privileged Auth Admin): manage role assignments and PIM settings.
These cover most operational needs without resorting to Global Admin.

Scoping Tools
- Administrative Units (AUs): group users/devices (e.g., by region or department) and scope role assignments to those subsets. A Helpdesk Admin scoped to the Sales AU can only reset Sales passwords.
- App- and Service-Specific RBAC: Exchange role groups, Teams policies, Intune RBAC let you restrict actions within each workload.
- Custom Roles: In Microsoft Entra ID, you can define custom roles with specific permissions when built-ins are too coarse.
- Conditional Access and Privileged Identity Management (PIM): CA protects sign-ins; PIM makes high privilege eligible (not always on), with approval, MFA, and time limits.

Safe Patterns
- Break-glass accounts: keep 2 emergency Global Admins with strong passwords, MFA exceptions only if documented compensating controls, and monitored sign-ins. Store credentials securely.
- Use Global Reader for visibility instead of handing out Global Admin.
- Prefer group-based role assignment for consistency and reviews; avoid one-off user assignments.
- Make powerful roles eligible in PIM with approval and time-bound activation.
- Scope roles to AUs or resource scopes where possible.
- Log and review: audit role activations and changes; run access reviews quarterly.

Assignment Workflow (Practical)
1) Define the task: what exact actions are needed? 2) Map to smallest fitting role. 3) Scope to the smallest set of objects (AU, site, team, device group). 4) Prefer eligible over permanent via PIM. 5) Require MFA and, for sensitive roles, approvals and ticket references. 6) Set a time limit. 7) Notify and log. 8) Review after the task, then remove or let eligibility lapse.

Common Pitfalls
- Role sprawl: too many overlapping roles; fix with a catalog and naming standards.
- Privilege creep: temporary access becomes permanent; fix with PIM and access reviews.
- Overuse of Global Admin: replace with targeted admin roles and readers.
- No scoping: tenant-wide permissions for local tasks; fix with AUs and per-workload RBAC.

Mental Model
Think of permissions as hazardous energy. Contain it (least privilege), time it (JIT), and monitor it (logs and reviews). Design roles around jobs-to-be-done, not people’s seniority.

Example

Temporary, scoped helpdesk: A contractor must reset passwords for the Sales department for two weeks. Create an Administrative Unit named Sales. Add Sales users to it. Assign the contractor to Helpdesk Administrator scoped to Sales AU, not tenant-wide. Use PIM to make the role eligible, with 8-hour activations requiring ticket ID and MFA. After two weeks, remove eligibility. Result: the contractor can’t touch HR or Engineering accounts, and access ends on schedule.

Scenario

An audit finds 14 Global Administrators in a 2,000-user tenant. You inventory what each person actually does: three manage Exchange, two handle Teams, two run Intune, two need only read access, and five rarely perform admin tasks. You propose: reduce Global Admins to 2 break-glass plus 1 operations lead (eligible via PIM). Move Exchange staff to Exchange Administrator, Teams staff to Teams Administrator, endpoint team to Intune Administrator, and give the two auditors Global Reader. For the five occasional admins, make them eligible for narrowly scoped roles in PIM with approvals. You set up quarterly access reviews and turn on alerts for any direct Global Admin assignments. The audit risk drops while daily work continues smoothly.

Reflection Question

How do you balance least privilege against the operational friction it can create, and where would you deliberately trade convenience for containment in your environment?

WISDOM
------

Topic

Signals vs Noise

Lesson

Core Idea
Signal is the meaningful pattern you care about. Noise is random fluctuation that looks like a pattern but isn’t. Progress depends on acting on signals and ignoring noise. The difficulty is that noise often feels urgent because it is loud, recent, or vivid.

Why Distinguishing Is Hard
Our brains love stories and hate uncertainty. We overreact to fresh anecdotes (recency bias), see patterns in small samples (law of small numbers), and chase confirmation. We also misread base rates: rare events feel common after a cluster. Without guardrails, we’ll optimize to yesterday’s blip and miss the long-term drift.

Useful Properties
- Variance vs trend: a noisy series wiggles; a trend drifts persistently. Look for direction sustained across time windows, not a single point.
- Regression to the mean: extreme highs and lows tend to be followed by more average values. Don’t assume a hot streak means a new normal.
- Lag and lead: some indicators move before outcomes (leading), others after (lagging). Judging today’s change with a lagging metric invites mistakes.

Simple Tools to Filter Noise
1) Widen the window: compare today not with yesterday, but with a 4–12-week moving average. If the average is flat while daily points jump, that’s likely noise.
2) Use baselines and bounds: define normal ranges (e.g., historical mean ± two standard deviations) so you can see when data truly leave the corridor.
3) Triangulate: require two independent indicators to move before you act (e.g., both conversion rate and qualified leads rise, not just traffic).
4) Precommit criteria: decide in advance what threshold triggers action, and how you’ll measure impact. This reduces impulse decisions.
5) Sample size sanity: small n magnifies noise. A survey of 20 is far noisier than 2,000. Treat early data as exploration, not proof.
6) Smoothing with caution: rolling averages and medians reduce noise; over-smoothing can hide real shifts. Match the window to decision cadence.

Decision Thresholds and Error Costs
Borrow from signal detection theory: you can miss a real signal (false negative) or act on noise (false positive). Choose thresholds based on which error costs more. In safety-critical contexts, tolerate more false alarms; in scarce-resource contexts, demand stronger evidence. Write this trade-off down.

Narrative Checks
Ask: what mechanism would produce this trend? Do external comparables show the same move? Can a single outlier explain it? If the story falls apart under a few “why” questions, you likely have noise.

Feedback Loops
Make small bets, measure, and iterate. Instead of reacting with a full pivot, run a limited test. If the effect persists under new data and different measurement, you’re seeing signal. If it vanishes, you’ve filtered noise cheaply.

Communication Habits
Report ranges and uncertainty, not single-point claims. Show the trendline alongside daily dots. Note sample sizes. Flag when a move is within normal bounds. This trains your team (and yourself) to think in distributions.

Mindset
Patience is not passivity. It is disciplined waiting for sufficient evidence. The goal isn’t to be unreactive; it’s to react proportionally to the quality of the signal.

Example

Daily step counts: One day you hit 16,000 steps; two days later only 3,500. If you react to the low day by buying a treadmill, you’re chasing noise. Instead, compute a 4-week average. If it trends from 7,200 to 7,600 to 7,800 over three weeks, that’s a slow, real improvement. Add one intervention at a time (e.g., schedule a 20-minute walk after lunch), then see if the 4-week average rises again. You’re anchoring on signal, not daily mood swings.

Scenario

Your startup launches a new onboarding flow. On day two, sign-ups jump 18%; on day three, they fall 12%. Support tickets are quiet, and conversion from email to signup is steady. You hold steady for two weeks, tracking a 7-day moving average of activation, first-payment rate, and support tickets. Activation’s moving average rises from 42% to 47%, first-payment nudges from 9.8% to 10.4%, and tickets remain flat. That combination—two independent metrics up, no harm signals—suggests a real improvement. You declare success and only then roll the change to all regions.

Reflection Question

For a decision you face, what is the smallest reliable signal you would accept before committing resources, and what are the costs of acting too early versus too late?

FICTION-WRITING
---------------

Topic

Scene vs Summary

Lesson

Core Distinction
A scene unfolds in real time. It shows action, dialogue, and sensory detail so the reader experiences events as they happen. A summary compresses time. It tells what happened across minutes, days, or years, focusing on outcomes and transitions. Both are essential tools. Scenes create immediacy and emotional stakes; summaries control pace, move us across time and space, and keep only the parts that matter onstage.

When to Use a Scene
Use a scene when something changes that affects the story’s trajectory: a decision, a confrontation, a revelation, a price paid. Scenes are best for moments with conflict, choice, risk, or surprise. They allow subtext in dialogue, physical beats that reveal character, and vivid setting that shapes action. Think of a scene as a lens you focus tightly on turning points.

When to Use a Summary
Use a summary to bridge, condense, or reframe. Travel, training montages, waiting periods, routine repetition, or background context are prime candidates. Summary also works for aftermath processing when dramatizing would be redundant. Good summary carries voice and perspective; it isn’t bland. It should have a purpose: to set up the next live moment or to highlight a change without belaboring it.

Scene–Sequel Rhythm
A classic pattern: a scene (goal, conflict, disaster) followed by a sequel (reaction, dilemma, decision). The sequel can be mostly summary—internal processing, time passing—while the next scene launches from the decision. This alternation keeps the narrative breathing: tension rises in scenes and resets with purposeful condensation.

Exposition Sliding Door
Exposition can slip into both scene and summary. The test: does this information change how we read the present moment? If yes, weave it into the scene as a beat or line of dialogue. If no, push it into a tight summary later, or cut it. You control the door: dramatize only what earns its keep.

Techniques for Strong Scenes
- Enter late, exit early: cut the warm-up and the wind-down.
- Anchor the want: open with what the POV character wants right now.
- Use concrete beats: gesture, setting, physical obstacles to externalize emotion.
- Layer subtext: let what’s unsaid do work.
- End with a shift: a new problem, decision, or revealed cost.

Techniques for Strong Summaries
- Decide the unit of time: a sentence, a paragraph, a page. Keep it tight.
- Choose a throughline: what changed across this span? Organize around that.
- Use selective detail: one or two specifics to avoid blandness.
- Maintain voice: let the narrator’s attitude color the condensation.
- Transition cleanly: signal entry (“Over the next week…”) and exit (“By Friday…”).

Common Pitfalls
- Over-summarizing crucial turns: if stakes hinge on it, show it.
- Over-scenifying routine: if nothing shifts, compress.
- Flat summary voice: inject perspective; avoid laundry lists.
- Info-dumps: embed needed facts inside action or purposeful summary.

Workflow
Outline beats first. Mark high-value turns for scenes. Mark connective tissue for summary. Draft scenes with tight focus; draft summaries with a clear change statement. On revision, convert flabby scenes to summary or expand skeletal summaries into scenes where needed. Balance is craft, not formula.

Example

Mini-scene then purposeful summary:
Scene: The cafe smelled like burnt sugar when Maya slid the letter across the table. “I got in,” she said, too casual for the way her fingers trembled. Leo’s smile arrived late. “New York?” he asked, as if there were other cities. She nodded. Outside, a bus sighed; inside, the espresso machine hissed. He traced the rim of his cup. “That’s… fast.” She waited for him to say he was proud, that he’d visit, that distance was just another map. Instead he said, “My lease is up in June.” The light from the window cut his face in half. She folded the letter. A choice, she realized, had already been made.
Summary: Over the next two weeks they spoke in bulleted lists—rent, boxes, timelines—practicalities that circled the thing they didn’t name. By Friday, his books were in crates, her letter thumbed soft at the crease. The apartment sounded bigger. Neither of them mentioned New York.
Purpose: The scene dramatizes the turning point; the summary compresses the quiet drift that follows, carrying voice and a clear change.

Scenario

You’re drafting a chapter where your protagonist travels across three towns to confront a mentor. The confrontation is the turning point—write that as a scene. The travel itself generates no new stakes—condense it into a paragraph of summary that shows how the landscape mirrors her mood and how her resolve firms with each mile. Then, when she arrives, drop into real time again. The pacing stays taut: no meandering carriage rides unless they change the story.

Reflection Question

In the story you’re writing, which recent passage feels slow or flat—what would happen if you converted its least essential pages into a tight summary and spent those words dramatizing the next actual turning point?
