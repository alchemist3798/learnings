FILM-PHOTOGRAPHY
----------------

Topic

The Exposure Triangle for Film

Lesson

First principles
Exposure is how much light reaches your film. Film does not see the world the way your eyes do. It collects light over time to create density on the negative or slide. You control the amount of light with three levers that always interact: shutter speed, aperture, and ISO. For film, ISO is set by the roll you loaded. Shutter and aperture are variable on every shot. The art is choosing a combination that fits your creative intent while giving the film the light it needs.

Shutter speed — time and motion
Shutter speed is the length of time the film is exposed. Faster speeds like 1 over 1000 let in less light and freeze motion. Slower speeds like 1 over 30 let in more light and blur motion. The practical constraint is camera shake. Handheld, a simple rule is the reciprocal of your focal length. With a 50 mm lens, try to stay at 1 over 60 or faster. Image stabilization and careful technique can stretch this, but it is a safe baseline.

Aperture — area and depth
Aperture is the opening in the lens that sets how much light passes through at once. It is written as f numbers like f2, f2.8, f4, f5.6, f8, f11, f16. Each full stop number lets in half as much light as the previous larger opening. Aperture also controls depth of field — how much of the scene appears in focus. Wide openings like f2 make shallow depth with a blurry background. Narrow openings like f11 make more of the scene appear sharp. Lens character also shows more at wider apertures — glow, vignetting, and bokeh quality.

ISO — baked-in sensitivity and grain
With film, ISO reflects the sensitivity of the emulsion and is fixed once loaded. ISO 100 is less sensitive and needs more light but gives fine grain and wide latitude in bright scenes. ISO 400 is more sensitive and needs less light, making it useful indoors or at dusk, with more visible grain. You can overexpose or underexpose on purpose and adjust in development — called pulling or pushing — but that changes contrast and grain. A simpler approach for negative film is to rate it a bit lower than box speed to give it extra light, which many labs and photographers prefer.

Stops and trade-offs
A stop is a doubling or halving of light. If you speed up the shutter one stop — say from 1 over 125 to 1 over 250 — you must open the aperture one stop — from f8 to f5.6 — to keep the same total light. Think of exposure as a budget that must balance. Want to freeze action. Spend your budget on a fast shutter, then open the aperture or choose a faster film to pay for it. Want deep focus. Spend your budget on a small aperture, then slow the shutter or use a tripod to pay for it.

Sunny 16 — a solid fallback
On a bright sunny day, set aperture to f16 and shutter speed to the reciprocal of your film ISO. With ISO 100 film, use 1 over 100 or 1 over 125 at f16. That is the baseline. Adjust for conditions in stops. Light haze or light cloud — open one stop to f11. Overcast — open two stops to f8. Open shade or backlit subject — open three stops to f5.6. Snow or beach sand that reflects lots of light — close down one stop to f22. Golden hour— even though the sun is out, the light is weaker and warmer, so expect to open 1 to 2 stops compared to midday.

Metering with film
Your in-camera meter measures reflected light and assumes the scene is middle gray. This can mislead you with very bright or very dark scenes. An incident meter that measures light falling on the subject is more reliable, but you can get close with judgment. If the background is bright sky and your subject is in shade, meter for the face by pointing the camera down to fill the frame with the face, lock the reading, then recompose. With negative film, slight overexposure is usually safer than underexposure. With slide film, be precise and protect the highlights.

A practical workflow
Start with intent. Do you care more about freezing motion or about how much is in focus. If motion matters, pick the slowest shutter that still freezes your subject, based on their speed and your lens — say 1 over 500 for fast action, 1 over 125 for casual movement. Then open or close aperture to match the meter. If depth of field matters, pick the aperture first — say f8 for landscapes — then adjust shutter to match. If the light is changing fast or your meter fails, fall back to Sunny 16 and adapt.

Common pitfalls to avoid
Do not forget that with film, ISO is not a dial you can spin freely. Choose your film to match your day. Watch for camera shake at slow speeds. Beware of backlight — the meter may underexpose faces. If you use strong neutral density or color filters, remember they reduce light and need compensation in stops. For exposures longer than one second, some films need extra time due to reciprocity failure — check your film data sheet if doing night work.

Mental model
Treat the exposure triangle like a three-way seesaw with a fixed total. Move one side for creative reasons, then move another to keep balance. Let intent lead, not the meter. The meter is a tool; your choice is the photograph.

Example

You are shooting a friend riding a bicycle past you at sunset with ISO 400 color negative film. Your intent is to freeze the rider but keep the glowing sky. You choose 1 over 500 to stop motion. The light is low, so Sunny 16 suggests that near sunset you are about three stops more open than midday. If midday would be 1 over 400 at f16, sunset is roughly 1 over 500 at f5.6. You set 1 over 500 and f5.6. To keep the sky from blowing out while still seeing the rider, you angle slightly so the sun is at the edge of the frame, meter off the rider’s face by filling the center with it, lock exposure, then recompose. Negative film tolerates a bit of overexposure, so this choice protects the rider and keeps sky color.

Scenario

At a family picnic you load ISO 200 film. Clouds pass over the sun. You want a group shot where both front row and background trees look sharp. You start with depth first and pick f8. You check Sunny 16: in light cloud you open one stop from f16 to f11, and in heavier cloud you open two stops to f8. Since you want f8 for depth, you set f8 and then choose a shutter near 1 over 200 — 1 over 250 is closest. A cloud thickens. You open your shutter one stop to 1 over 125 to keep exposure, keeping f8 locked for depth. When the sun returns, you go back to 1 over 250. The triangle balances around your fixed choice of f8.

Reflection Question

When the light is imperfect, what single creative choice — freezing motion, blurring motion, or controlling depth — matters most to your intent, and how will you bend the other two sides of the triangle to serve it?

MICROECONOMICS
--------------

Topic

Incentives and Human Behavior

Lesson

First principles
People respond to incentives, not to wishes. An incentive changes the payoff of choosing one action over another. Behavior moves at the margin — the next choice — where perceived benefits exceed perceived costs, given constraints like time, information, and norms. Good incentive design starts with a clear target behavior and an honest model of how real people trade off money, time, status, risk, and identity under uncertainty.

Types of incentives
Monetary incentives pay or charge money — wages, prices, bonuses, fines. Social incentives move status, approval, or belonging — public recognition, shame, leaderboards. Moral incentives appeal to values, duties, and self-image — pride in doing the right thing, guilt for harm. Intrinsic motivation is the satisfaction from the activity itself. Extrinsic incentives come from outside. Either can support the other — or crowd it out if misaligned.

Design goals
A well-designed incentive makes the desired action feel easier, sooner, and safer than the alternatives. It makes the undesired action feel harder, later, and riskier. It is legible — people understand what earns what. It is robust to gaming. It anticipates substitution — people switching to a close alternative that avoids the penalty or captures the reward without the intended effort.

Second-order effects
Every incentive invites a workaround. Measure output only — people may cut corners on quality. Reward zero accidents — people may hide incidents. Fine lateness at a daycare — some parents may treat the fine as a fee and arrive later. Pay for each task — people may cherry-pick easy tasks and ignore difficult ones. This is Goodhart’s law — when a measure becomes a target, it can stop being a good measure. The cobra effect is when people breed cobras for a bounty and release them when the program ends, worsening the problem. Risk compensation happens when safety devices make people behave more recklessly. Always ask — what will smart, busy, self-interested people do next.

Framework for incentive design
Who exactly is the decision maker. What is the marginal choice you want to shift. How will they perceive costs and benefits in the moment. How could they game it. What substitutes might they switch to. What time horizon matters to them — now, this week, this quarter. How will you measure success without distorting core quality. What guardrails limit harm — caps, audits, paired measures, and sunset dates. Where can you add a social or moral signal to reinforce the monetary nudge.

Common patterns that work
Combine types. A small cash bonus paired with public praise often beats a larger cash-only bonus. Reward good leading indicators, not just outcomes — for safety, reward near-miss reports and training completion. Pay for improvement, not just level — avoids penalizing already high performers. Use loss framing — people work harder to avoid losing a reward they feel they already own. Make the desired action the default — auto-enroll in retirement plans boosts participation far more than raising match rates.

Fairness and trust
People are not calculators. Perceived fairness matters. A small, transparent, predictable plan can outperform a complex, rich plan that feels arbitrary. If rules change midstream or the organization pays lip service to values while rewarding the opposite, behavior follows the real incentives and morale erodes. Align stories with paychecks.

Testing and iteration
Start small. Pilot with a subset. Pre-mortem the ways it could backfire. Instrument the system — track main outcomes and side effects. Talk to participants about how they made choices. Adjust. Be ready to turn it off if it distorts behavior.

Mental model
Think of behavior as water flowing downhill. Incentives shape the slope. Lower the path you want. Raise the ridge on paths you do not want. Watch where the water goes next and reshape again.

Cautions
Do not pay for what you cannot verify. Do not rely on a single metric. Do not punish honest reporting. Do not promise what you will not consistently deliver. Do not forget that even small frictions — extra clicks, forms, or delays — are powerful negative incentives.

Example

A company pays a quarterly bonus if a team reports zero safety incidents. Incidents drop, but injury severity at the hospital rises and whispers suggest underreporting. Redesign: remove the zero-incident bonus. Instead, award points for each near-miss report, each safety walk, and each fix completed, with public recognition at standups. Severe incidents trigger a blameless review where contributors earn points for transparent reporting and corrective actions. Pair with a small team budget for safety improvements chosen by the workers. Reporting spikes, minor hazards are removed faster, and severe injuries decline.

Scenario

A city wants to cut downtown congestion. Option A is a daily driving fee. Option B keeps streets free but gives transit credits to downtown workers, with discounts rising if they ride three days in a week. The city also nudges employers to offer preferred parking to carpools and makes the transit card the default employee benefit unless workers opt out. City staff monitors not only traffic counts but also spillover parking in adjacent neighborhoods and small business sales. In the pilot, the credit plus default enrollment and carpool perks reduce solo driving without harming nearby shops, so the plan scales citywide.

Reflection Question

When might paying or penalizing for an outcome you care about actually reduce the true quality of that outcome, and how would you redesign the incentive to reward the behaviors that produce lasting value instead?

MICROSOFT 365 ADMINISTRATOR (MS-102)
------------------------------------

Topic

Navigating Microsoft 365 admin portals

Lesson

First principles
Admin work in Microsoft 365 is wayfinding. You are moving through a connected set of portals that each manage one slice of the tenant — identity, mail, collaboration, security, devices, and apps. The core skill is building a mental map so you can predict which portal holds which settings, and how to jump there fast. Roles matter — what you can see depends on your admin role. Search is your compass — both in-portal search and global documentation search.

The home base — Microsoft 365 admin center
Start at admin.microsoft.com. This is the home for tenant overview, users and groups, licenses and billing, service health, and organization-wide settings. It is also your launch pad — the left navigation includes entry points to workload admin centers like Exchange, SharePoint, and Teams. Use the global search bar to find common tasks — create user, assign license, reset password, domain setup. Bookmark the Org settings and Settings search to quickly find things like security defaults, external sharing, and integrated apps.

Identity and access — Microsoft Entra admin center
Go to entra.microsoft.com. Here you manage users, groups, roles, sign-in logs, Conditional Access, multifactor authentication, enterprise apps, and identity governance. If it is about who someone is, how they sign in, and what conditions apply, start here. Tip — MFA configuration has multiple homes. Security defaults in Microsoft 365 admin turn on a baseline. For granular control use Conditional Access in Entra. Application single sign-on also lives here under Enterprise applications.

Email and calendaring — Exchange admin center
Visit admin.exchange.microsoft.com. Manage mailboxes, shared mailboxes, distribution lists, transport rules, retention, mobile device access via Exchange ActiveSync, and mail flow troubleshooting. If you are creating shared mailboxes, setting forwarding, or diagnosing non-delivery reports, this is the place. Many organization-wide mail hygiene features surface in security.microsoft.com, but mailbox-level settings are here.

Collaboration content — SharePoint and OneDrive admin
From the Microsoft 365 admin center, open the SharePoint admin center. Manage site collections, sharing policies, storage limits, and OneDrive settings. If the question is where files live and who can share them, look here. External sharing has layers — tenant-level in SharePoint admin, site-level per site, and link-level when creating a share. Knowing this hierarchy speeds troubleshooting.

Meetings and chat — Teams admin center
Go to admin.teams.microsoft.com. Configure meeting policies, messaging policies, voice, emergency addresses, and app permissions. Teams inherits identity from Entra and files from SharePoint, so think of Teams as the orchestration layer. If a team cannot be created, check naming policies in Entra. If files will not share, check SharePoint external sharing first.

Security and compliance — Microsoft Defender and Purview
Security center at security.microsoft.com brings together Defender for Office, identity signals, endpoint security, and incident response. The compliance hub at compliance.microsoft.com — Microsoft Purview — manages retention, data loss prevention, eDiscovery, sensitivity labels, and insider risk. If the request mentions retention, labeling, or legal hold, start in Purview. If it mentions phishing, malware, or investigations, start in Defender.

Devices and apps — Microsoft Intune and Power Platform
Device and app management lives at intune.microsoft.com. Here you enroll devices, deploy apps, and set configuration profiles and compliance policies. Low-code app environment and governance is at admin.powerplatform.microsoft.com. If someone cannot enroll a phone, think Intune. If an app environment needs DLP rules, think Power Platform admin plus Purview.

Wayfinding patterns
Ask what the object is — user, group, mailbox, site, team, device, app, or policy — and that points to the portal. Use the portal search bar — type mailbox, conditional access, retention, or device compliance and jump straight in. Follow deep links from Microsoft Learn docs — they land you on the exact blade. If you get access denied, you likely need a specific admin role. Assign least privilege — Exchange admin rather than Global admin when possible.

Troubleshooting flow
Reproduce the issue with a test user. Check service health. Locate the object in its home portal. Review applied policies — Conditional Access, retention, sharing, meeting. Check audit or sign-in logs. Trace dependencies — Teams uses SharePoint and Exchange. Make one change at a time, wait for policy propagation, and retest.

Mental model
Think of the tenant as a city. Microsoft 365 admin center is city hall. Entra is the identity registry and border control. Exchange is the post office. SharePoint is the library and records center. Teams is the town square. Defender is the police, Purview is the courthouse, and Intune is the vehicle and device garage. When you know which building handles a request, navigation is quick.

Example

Create a shared mailbox for info at your company and add two users as members. Path — In Microsoft 365 admin center, select Exchange admin center. Go to Recipients then Mailboxes then Shared. Create a new shared mailbox named Info with address info at your domain. After creation, open the mailbox properties and add User A and User B as members so they can read and send as the mailbox. Back in Microsoft 365 admin center, confirm the shared mailbox appears under Teams and groups if needed. Test by sending an email to info and verifying both users can see and reply from it in Outlook.

Scenario

A new hire starts tomorrow. You need to create the account, assign a license, enforce MFA, enable email, and get them into the Sales team. Flow — In Microsoft 365 admin center, create the user and assign a Microsoft 365 E3 license. In Entra admin center, check that the user is in the correct groups and that Conditional Access puts them into MFA. If you use security defaults instead, confirm they will be prompted for MFA at first sign-in. In Exchange admin center, verify the user mailbox exists and set a mailbox policy if needed. In Teams admin center, add the user to the Sales team and confirm meeting policy assignment. Send the welcome steps and verify sign-in from a test device.

Reflection Question

When you do not know where a setting lives, what three clues about the problem — the object, the action, and the policy type — will you use to deduce the right portal, and why?

WISDOM
------

Topic

Reflection and Journaling

Lesson

First principles
Experience does not teach by itself. Reflection converts experience into insight you can reuse. A simple loop drives growth — do, notice, name, adjust, do again. Journaling is just a lightweight tool to run this loop on purpose. The goal is not a beautiful notebook. The goal is better judgment tomorrow, earned by looking at today with steady eyes.

Make it brief and reliable
Short beats perfect. A five-minute entry you do daily outperforms an epic once a month. Design a tiny ritual you can keep even on hard days. Tie it to a trigger you already do — after brushing teeth, after your commute, or before shutting the laptop. Put the journal where you will use it. Lower friction until starting feels silly not to do.

A clear structure
Use a small, repeatable template so your brain knows what to do. Try this 5 by 1 format.
1. Facts — one to three bullet points on what actually happened, with no judgment.
2. Feelings — one line naming the strongest emotion you felt.
3. Lessons — one sentence of what you learned or would do differently.
4. Next tiny step — one action you can take in less than ten minutes.
5. Gratitude or win — one thing that went right, however small.
This keeps you honest, kind, and forward moving in five lines.

Capture and clarity
Write in simple, plain language. Short bullets. Fragments welcome. Avoid long justifications. If you notice a mental movie starting, pause and return to facts. If your mind insists on arguing, set a three-minute timer and then stop. The aim is to see, not to litigate.

Prompts that work
When stuck, use prompts that tilt you toward agency and learning. What mattered most today and why. Where did I feel friction. What did I avoid and what is the first five-minute step toward it. What surprised me. What would future me thank me for doing tomorrow. What belief did today test.

From notes to principles
Once a week, skim the last seven entries. Star any repeated lessons. Boil them down to short principles — one-liners you can act on. Examples — schedule hard tasks before noon, say no by default to unscoped requests, ask for examples before giving advice. Keep a living page for these and look at it before planning your week. This turns journal noise into guidance.

Handling heavy emotions
When the day is rough, start with the body — I felt my chest tighten when that email came. Name feelings without arguing about their legitimacy. Then ask — what need is unmet here. Respect, clarity, rest, connection. Pick one kind action that meets that need even a little. Send a clarifying note. Take a ten-minute walk. Book a call. Reflection without care can become rumination. The antidote is a small, compassionate next step.

Privacy and tools
Use whatever tool you will actually use — paper, a plain text note, voice memo, or a simple app. Protect privacy — a locked note, a notebook you store well, or a digital file with encryption. Back it up. Do not confuse gear with practice. One pen and one place are enough.

Common pitfalls
Waiting for big insights. The gold is in small, repeated observations. Ranting without deciding a next step. Writing for an imaginary audience. Overengineering tags and templates before you have a habit. Skipping review — reflection compounds only if you loop back and distill.

Mental model
Treat journaling as maintenance for your decision engine. You would not skip changing oil because the last drive felt fine. Five minutes a day keeps the parts clean, the dials clear, and the road ahead visible.

Example

A five-minute entry at day’s end. Facts — Pushed code review back twice; ran 20 minutes; had tense chat with Sam about timelines. Feelings — Anxious and a bit defensive. Lessons — I avoid reviews when I am not clear on scope; asking for examples early would help. Next tiny step — Message Sam now to ask for two recent examples of done for this feature. Gratitude or win — Even though I wanted to skip it, I ran and felt better after.

Scenario

You are a new manager and had a difficult meeting where a stakeholder questioned your team’s estimates. After the meeting, you open your journal. Facts — Stakeholder said estimates were padded; I explained risk buffer; I did not have last project’s variance data handy. Feelings — Embarrassed, protective of the team. Lessons — I need a standard slide with past variance and a one-minute explanation of buffers. Next tiny step — Block 15 minutes tomorrow morning to pull variance numbers and draft the slide. Gratitude or win — Team stayed calm and offered to share unknowns. The next day, you open the meeting with the new slide; the tone shifts from debate to plan.

Reflection Question

What single question, if answered honestly at the end of each day, would most improve your decisions next week — and how can you make answering it so easy you will not skip it?

FICTION-WRITING
---------------

Topic

Character Basics: Goal, Stakes, and Conflict

Lesson

First principles
Stories move when someone wants something specific, cannot get it easily, and risks losing something that matters. Goal, stakes, and conflict are the engine of momentum. Without a clear goal, scenes wander. Without meaningful stakes, readers do not care. Without real conflict, effort feels weightless. Nail these three and everything else has a frame to hang on.

Goal — specific and visible
A strong goal is external, concrete, and measurable. Win the scholarship. Rescue the dog before the storm hits. Land a deal by Friday. It is also personally meaningful — not a task assigned by the plot, but something the character themselves must have. The goal tells you what success looks like in a scene and what the character will try next.

Stakes — why it matters
Stakes answer so what. They are the lived cost of failure and the cost of pursuit. There are outer stakes — money, job, freedom, safety — and inner stakes — identity, love, self-respect, belonging. The best stakes are specific and personal. Not just lose the business, but have to sell the grandmother’s bakery and scatter the staff who became family. Stakes should escalate as the story unfolds, tightening time, raising costs, and forcing choices.

Conflict — opposition with teeth
Conflict is not just arguing. It is force that resists the goal. It can be external — an antagonist, a system, nature, a ticking clock — and internal — fear, misbelief, guilt, competing desires. The richest conflict braids the two. The world pushes back while the character’s own flaw or wound makes the pushback harder to overcome. Conflict should force trade-offs; the character cannot have it all.

Internal vs external conflict
External conflict makes the story visible — a storm, a rival, a rule, a locked door. Internal conflict makes it human — a need to please, fear of abandonment, pride, shame. Map the link. A character who believes they are only lovable when perfect will sabotage a messy but honest alliance with the person who could help them win. That is story fuel.

Try–fail cycles
Readers invest when effort costs something. Give your character a plan; let it fail or half-succeed and extract a price; force a new plan. With each cycle, raise the stakes, shorten the time, or deepen the internal cost. Each failure should teach something and corner the character into revealing who they are through choices, not speeches.

Story problem statement
Compress your premise into one sentence you can test scenes against. When the inciting incident happens, protagonist must achieve goal before time pressure or looming consequence, or else stakes, but conflict stands in the way. Example — After losing their head chef, Maya must earn a Michelin star in 30 days to keep her restaurant, or she will lose the staff who became her family, but an obsessive need for control and a vengeful ex-partner undermine every service.

Practical steps
Write your character’s external goal in one line. Write the inner need or fear that will get in the way. Write outer stakes as a specific loss and inner stakes as a personal cost. Draft three try–fail beats that escalate. Check that in each beat the choice reveals values. Finally, scan scenes and cut anything that does not either advance the goal, raise the stakes, or sharpen the conflict.

Common pitfalls
Vague goals like be happier lead to vague scenes. Generic stakes like save the world without a personal anchor feel hollow. Conflict that relies on coincidence or simple misunderstandings frustrates. A protagonist who only reacts reads passive; ensure they make hard choices, even bad ones, to pursue what they want.

Mental model
Think of goal as a magnet pulling forward, stakes as the cliff edge behind, and conflict as the wind and terrain between. When all three are strong, every step feels necessary and earned.

Example

A junior lawyer, Lena, wants to win an asylum case for a client before a deportation hearing in 10 days. External goal — secure legal relief. Internal conflict — Lena fears public failure and avoids risky arguments, stemming from a past courtroom mistake. Stakes — if she fails, the client is sent back to danger; if she holds back, she betrays her vow to use her privilege to protect others. Conflict — an ambitious colleague sabotages shared evidence to take the win, and Lena’s fear of bold strategy makes her second-guess the only argument that could work. Try–fail beats — 1 Lena files a safe motion; it is denied. 2 She confronts the colleague; they leak a rumor that shakes the client’s trust. 3 She commits to the bold argument and risks her reputation; the judge challenges her, forcing Lena to reveal the personal story behind her conviction, which turns the tide.

Scenario

You are outlining a cozy mystery. Protagonist — Noor, a bookshop owner. Goal — clear her brother of suspicion in a theft at the town museum before the festival opens in seven days. External conflict — the museum board blocks access and a rival shop owner spreads rumors. Internal conflict — Noor avoids confrontation to keep the peace, a habit from growing up as the fixer in her family. Stakes — if she fails, her brother faces charges, their shop’s reputation collapses, and Noor confirms her fear that speaking up harms those she loves. You outline three try–fail cycles — Noor follows a polite lead that wastes time; she tries to bargain for access and is rebuffed publicly; she chooses to confront the rival and risks a social rift, which finally shakes loose the clue that clears her brother — but also reveals a deeper theft that points to someone she admires.

Reflection Question

What would your protagonist fear losing so much that they would break one of their own rules to protect it — and how can you design a choice that forces that risk into the open?
